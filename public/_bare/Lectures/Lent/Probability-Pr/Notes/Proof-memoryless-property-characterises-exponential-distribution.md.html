First if $T\sim E(\lambda)$, then $$ \begin{align*} \prob(T > t) = \int_{t}^{\infty} \lambda e^{-\lambda s} \, ds = e^{\lambda t} \end{align*} $$ so for $s,t > 0$, $$ \begin{align*} \prob(T>s+t | T>s) = \frac{e^{-\lambda(s+t)}}{e^{-\lambda s}} = e^{-\lambda t} = \prob(T > t). \end{align*} $$

Now suppose a random variable $T$ satisfies $\prob(T>s+t) = \prob(T>s) \prob(T>t)$ and is not trivially identically to $0$ or $\infty$. For $t\geq 0$ and $m\in\N$, by induction, $$ \begin{align*} \prob(T > mt) = \prob(T > t)^{m}, \end{align*} $$ and for all $n\in\N$, $$ \begin{align*} \prob(T > \frac{m}{n})^{n} = \prob(T > m) = \prob(T > 1)^{m} \implies \prob(T > \frac{m}{n}) = \prob(T > 1)^{m/n}. \end{align*} $$ Now by the assumption that $T$ is not trivial we have $\prob(T > 1) \in (0, 1)$, so let $$ \begin{align*} \lambda = - \log \prob(T>1) \in (0,\infty). \end{align*} $$ Then $$ \begin{align*} \prob(T>t) = e^{-\lambda t} \quad \forall t\in\Q. \end{align*} $$ But both sides are non-increasing in $t$, so this extends to all $t\geq 0$ so $T\sim E(\lambda)$.
