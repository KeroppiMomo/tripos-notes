Let $p_n$ be the number of permutations of a set of size $n$, and let $s_{n,k}$ be the number of subsets of $\set{1,2,\ldots,n}$ of size $k$. We want to show $p_n = s_{n,k} p_k p_{n-k}$.

Let $\Omega_n$ for the set of permutations of $\set{1,2,\ldots,n}$. For some subset $S\subseteq \set{1,2,\ldots,n}$ of size $k$, let $$ \Omega_n^S = \sb{\sigma\in S_n}{\sigma(\set{1,2,\ldots,k}) = S}. $$ Since every $\sigma\in S_n$ maps $\set{1,2,\ldots,k}$ to some subset of size $k$, $$ \Omega_n = \bigcup_{S\subseteq\set{1,2,\ldots,n}} \Omega_n^S. $$

For some $S\subseteq\set{1,2,\ldots,n}$, given some $\sigma\in\Omega_n^S$, let $$\begin{align*} \sigma_1:& &\set{1,2,\ldots,k}&\to S \\ \sigma_2:& &\set{k+1, k+2, \ldots, n}&\to \set{1,2,\ldots,n}\setminus S \end{align*}$$ be the restrictions $\sigma$ to the respective domains. Note that $\sigma_1,\sigma_2$ are bijections and we obtain all pairs of bijection in this way.

Therefore we have a bijection $$\begin{align*} \Omega_n^S&\to\set{\text{bijections } \set{1,\ldots,k}\to S}\times\set{\text{bijections }\set{k+1, \ldots,n}\to\set{1,\ldots,n}\setminus S} \\ \sigma&\mapsto(\sigma_1, \sigma_2) \end{align*}$$ so $$ |\Omega_n^S| = p_k p_{n-k}. $$

Since $\Omega_n^S$ for all $S$ are disjoint, $$\begin{align*} |\Omega_n| &= \sum_{S\subseteq\set{1,\ldots,n}} p_kp_{n-k} \\ p_n &= s_{n,k} p_k p_{n-k}. \end{align*}$$

Now, clearly $p_1=1$ and $s_{n,1}=n$. So $p_n = np_{n-1}$ and by induction, $p_n=n!$. Also $$ s_{n,k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}. $$
