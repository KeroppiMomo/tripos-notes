We see from binomial expansion $$ f(t) \equiv (1-t)^N = \fsum k0N (-1)^k \pmat{N\\k} t^k $$ that by taking $t=1$, $$ \begin{align*} \sum_{k=0}^N (-1)^k \pmat{N\\k} &= \begin{cases} 0, &N\geq 1 \\ 1, &N=0 \end{cases} \\ &= 1_{\set{N=0}}. \end{align*} $$

We'll take $N = \fsum i1n 1_{A_i}$. Notice for $k=0,1,\ldots, n$, $$ \sum_{1\leq i_1<\ldots<i_k\leq n} 1_{A_{i_1}\cap\ldots\cap A_{i_k}} = \pmat{N \\ k}. $$ (Here's an example to aid understanding: suppose $n=4$, $k=2$ and $\omega\in A_1\cap A_2\cap A_3\cap A_4^\complement$. $N(\omega) = 3$, and the non-zero terms on LHS are $1_{A_1\cap A_2} + 1_{A_1\cap A_3} + 1_{A_2\cap A_3}$.)

Then $$\begin{align*} 1_{(A_1\cup A_2\cup\ldots\cup A_n)^\complement} &= 1_{\set{N=0}} \\ &= \fsum k0N (-1)^k \pmat{N \\ k} \\ &= \fsum k0N (-1)^k \sum_{1\leq i_1<\ldots<i_k\leq n} 1_{{A_{i_1}}\cap\ldots\cap A_{i_k}} \\ &= \fsum k0n (-1)^k \sum_{1\leq i_1<\ldots<i_k\leq n} 1_{{A_{i_1}}\cap\ldots\cap A_{i_k}} & &\text{since the sum vanishes when $k>n$.} \end{align*}$$

Taking expectations on both sides and applying linearity, $$ \begin{align*} 1 - \prob(A_1\cup\ldots\cup A_n) &= \fsum k0n (-1)^k \sum_{1\leq i_1<\ldots<i_k\leq n} \prob(A_{i_1}\cap\ldots\cap A_{i_k}) \\ &= 1 - \fsum k1n (-1)^{k+1} \sum_{1\leq i_1<\ldots<i_k\leq n} \prob(A_{i_1}\cap\ldots\cap A_{i_k}) \\ \prob(A_1\cup\ldots\cup A_n) &= \fsum k1n (-1)^{k+1} \sum_{1\leq i_1<\ldots<i_k\leq n} \prob(A_{i_1}\cap\ldots\cap A_{i_k}) \\ \end{align*} $$ as required.
