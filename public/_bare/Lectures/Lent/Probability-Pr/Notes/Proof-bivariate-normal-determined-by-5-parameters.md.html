We always have $$ \begin{align*} V = \pmat{\sigma_1^2 & \sigma_1\sigma_2\rho \\ \sigma_1\sigma_2\rho & \sigma_2^2 }. \end{align*} $$ We just have to show that $V$ is positive definite iff $\rho\in(-1,1)$.

If $V$ is positive definite, we have $$ \begin{align*} 0 < \pmat{\sigma_2 & \pm\sigma_1} V \pmat{\sigma_2 \\ \pm\sigma_1} = 2\sigma_1^2\sigma_2^2 \pm 2\sigma_1^2\sigma_2^2\rho \end{align*} $$ so $\rho\in(-1, 1)$.

If $\rho\in(-1,1)$, then for all $(x_1, x_2)$, $$ \begin{align*} \pmat{x_1 & x_2} V \pmat{x_1 \\ x_2} &= x_1^2\sigma_1^2 + x_2^2\sigma_2^2 + 2x_1 x_2\sigma_1\sigma_2 \rho \\ &= \rho(\sigma_1x_1+\sigma_2x_2)^2 + (1-\rho)(\sigma_1^2x_1^2 + \sigma_2^2x_2^2) \tag{1} \\ &= -\rho(\sigma_1x_1+\sigma_2x_2)^2 + (1+\rho)(\sigma_1^2x_1^2 + \sigma_2^2x_2^2) \tag{2} \\ \end{align*} $$ Use (1) when $\rho\geq 0$ and (2) when $\rho\leq 0$ to see that it is positive.
