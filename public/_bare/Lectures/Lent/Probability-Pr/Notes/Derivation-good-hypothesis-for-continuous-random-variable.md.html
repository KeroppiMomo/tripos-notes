Suppose $F_X$ is piecewise continuously differentiable with derivative $f$. Then by the fundamental theorem of calculus, for $a\leq b$, $$ \begin{align*} \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx = F_X(b) - F_X(a), \end{align*} $$ so $f$ is a density function of $X$.

Suppose $X$ has a density function $f_X$ that is continuous at $x$. Then for all $h$, $$ \begin{align*} \left|\frac{F_X(x+h)-F_X(x)}{h} - f_X(x)\right| &= \frac{1}{|h|}\left|\int_{x}^{x+h} (f_X(y) - f_X(x)) \, dy\right| \\ &\leq \sup_{x\leq y\leq x+h} |f_X(y) - f_X(x)| \\ &\to 0. \end{align*} $$ So $F_X$ is differentiable at $x$ with $F_X'(x) = f_X(x)$.
