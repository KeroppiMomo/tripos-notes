The density function of $\v Z$ is, due to independence of $Z_1, \ldots, Z_n$, $$ \begin{align*} f_\v Z(\v z) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} f_{Z_i}(z_i) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z_i^2/2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{(2\pi)^n}} e^{-|\v z|^2/2}. \end{align*} $$

When $\matr V$ is positive definite, its eigenvalues are all positive. Then $\matr \sigma$ is invertible. So we can invert $$ \begin{align*} \v x = \matr\sigma \v z + \v \mu \iff \v z = \matr\sigma\inv(\v x-\v \mu). \end{align*} $$ The Jacobian is $$ \begin{align*} J = \pdfd{\v z}{\v x} = \det \matr\sigma\inv = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\det \matr V}}. \end{align*} $$ By a change of variable, $$ \begin{align*} f_\v X(\v x) = f_\v Z(\v z) |J| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{(2\pi)^n \det \matr V}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2} |\v z|^2\right). \end{align*} $$ We further find $|\v z|^2$ in terms of $\v x$ as $$ \begin{align*} |\v z|^2 &= \v z^T \v z \\ &= (\v x-\v \mu)^T (\matr\sigma\inv)^T \matr\sigma\inv (\v x-\v \mu) \\ &= (\v x-\v \mu)^T \matr V\inv (\v x-\v \mu) &&\text{by }\matr V=\matr \sigma\matr \sigma^T. \end{align*} $$ Substituting gives the result.
