Consider the probability space $\Omega = \set{0,1}^n$ with probability measure $\prob$ given by mass function $$ p_\omega = \for\prod k1n p^{\omega_k} (1-p)^{1-\omega_k}\quad\text{where } \omega=(\omega_1,\omega_2, \ldots, \omega_n). $$ Let random variables $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ given by $$ X_k(\omega) = \omega_k. $$

Notice that for all $x_1, \ldots, x_n\in\set{0,1}$, $$ \prob(X_1 = x_1, \ldots, X_n = x_n) = \for\prod k1n p^{x_k}(1-p)^{1-x_k} = \prob(X_1=x_1)\times\ldots\times\prob(X_n=x_n) $$ so $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent Bernoulli random variables.

Let $S = X_1 + X_2 + \ldots + X_n$. For $k=0,1,\ldots,n$, we have $|\set{S_n = k}| = \pmat{n\\k}$ and $p_\omega = p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$ for all $\omega\in\set{S_n=k}$. Therefore $$ \prob(S_n = k) = \pmat{n\\k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} $$ and hence $S_n\sim B(n,p)$.
