Assume $\Omega$ is countable. By finding an enumeration, $\Omega=\set{1,2,\ldots,N}$ for some $N\in\N$ or $\Omega=\N$.

To prove uniqueness, let $X\in\mathcal F^+$. Note that we have $$ X = \sum_n X_n \quad\text{where } X_n(\omega) =X(n)\,1_{\set{n}}(\omega). $$ If $\expect$ satisfies 1, 2, 3, then $$\begin{align*} \expect(X) &= \expect\Big(\sum_n X_n\Big) \\ &= \sum_n \expect(X_n) \\ &= \sum_n X(n)\,\expect\big(1_{\set{n}}\big) \\ &= \sum_n X(n)\,\prob(\set{n}) \\ &= \sum_{\omega\in\Omega} X(\omega)\,\prob(\set{\omega}). \tag{*} \end{align*}$$ Hence there is at most one such map $\expect$.

To prove existence, define $\expect(X)$ using (*).

  1. $\expect(1_A) = \sum_{\omega\in\Omega} 1_A(\omega)\,\prob(\set{\omega}) = \prob(A)$;

  2. For $\lambda>0$ and $X\in\mathcal F^+$, we have $$\expect(\lambda X) = \sum_{\omega\in\Omega} (\lambda X)(\omega)\,\prob(\set{\omega}) = \lambda\sum_{\omega\in\Omega} X(\omega)\,\prob(\set{\omega}) = \lambda\prob(X).$$

  3. For any sequence $(X_n)_{n\in\N}$ in $\mathcal F^+$, $$\expect\Big(\sum_n X_n\Big) = \sum_{\omega\in\Omega} \prob(\set{\omega}) \bigg(\sum_n X_n(\omega)\bigg) = \sum_n\sum_{\omega\in\Omega} X_n(\omega)\,\prob({\omega}) = \sum_n\expect(X_n)$$ since order of summation is interchangeable if the summands are non-negative.
