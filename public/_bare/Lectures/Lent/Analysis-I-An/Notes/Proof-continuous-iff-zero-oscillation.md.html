Suppose $f$ is continuous at $x$. Fix $\delta>0$. There exists $\epsilon>0$ such that for all $y\in(x-\epsilon, x+\epsilon)$, $$ \begin{align*} -\delta < f(y) - f(x) < \delta. \end{align*} $$ Then $$ \begin{align*} \inf_{(x-\epsilon, x+\epsilon)} f \geq -\delta, \qquad \sup_{(x-\epsilon, x+\epsilon)} f \leq \delta \end{align*} $$ so $\omega_f((x-\epsilon, x+\epsilon)) \leq 2\delta$. Therefore $\omega_f(x) \equiv \lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \omega_f((x-\epsilon, x+\epsilon)) = 0$.

Conversely suppose $\omega_f(x) = 0$. Fix $\delta>0$. Since $\omega_f((x-\epsilon, x+\epsilon))$ decreases as $\epsilon$ decreases, there exists $\epsilon>0$ such that $$ \begin{align*} \sup_{(x-\epsilon, x+\epsilon)} f - \inf_{(x-\epsilon, x+\epsilon)} f = \omega_f((x-\epsilon, x+\epsilon)) < \delta. \end{align*} $$ We have $\inf_{(x-\epsilon, x+\epsilon)} f \leq f(y) \leq \sup_{(x-\epsilon, x+\epsilon)} f$ for all $y\in(x-\epsilon, x+\epsilon)$, so $|f(y) - f(x)| < \delta$. Hence $f$ is continuous at $x$.
