For $0\leq x\leq 2$, $$ \begin{align*} \sin x = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{x^{4n-3}}{(4n-3)!}\left(1-\frac{x^2}{(4n-2)(4n-1)}\right) \geq 0 \end{align*} $$ and if $0< x \leq 2$, $\sin x>0$. So $\cos' x = -\sin x < 0$ for $0<x<2$ and hence $\cos x$ is strictly decreasing in this interval. Therfefore $\cos x$ has at most one root in $[0,2]$.

To complete the proof, we will show that $\cos\sqrt2 > 0$ and $\cos\sqrt3 < 0$. Then we apply the intermediate value theorem to show that a root exists in $[\sqrt2, \sqrt3]$.

Now $$ \begin{align*} \cos\sqrt2 &= \ub{1 - \frac{(\sqrt2)^2}{2!}}_{0} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(\sqrt2)^{4n}}{(4n)!}\ub{\left(1-\frac{(\sqrt2)^2}{(4n+1)(4n+2)}\right)}_{>0} > 0 \end{align*} $$ and $$ \begin{align*} \cos\sqrt3 &= \ub{1 - \frac{(\sqrt3)^2}{2!} + \frac{(\sqrt3)^{4}}{4!}}_{-1/8} - \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(\sqrt3)^{4n+2}}{(4n+2)!}\ub{\left(1 - \frac{(\sqrt3)^2}{(4n+3)(4n+4)}\right)}_{>0} < 0. \end{align*} $$

$\sin \pi/2 = 1$ follows from $\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1$.
