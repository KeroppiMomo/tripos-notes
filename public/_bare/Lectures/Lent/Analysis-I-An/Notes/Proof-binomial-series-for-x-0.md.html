Suppose $0\leq x<1$.

Clearly $$ f^{(n)}(x) = r(r-1)\ldots(r-n+1)(1+x)^{r-n}. $$

By Taylor's theorem with Lagrange remainder, for $n\geq r$, $$ (1+x)^r = 1+\pmat{r\\1}x + \ldots + \pmat{r\\n-1}x^{n-1} + \ub{\pmat{r\\n}\frac{x^n}{(1+\theta x)^{n-r}}}_{R_n(x)}$$ for some $\theta\in(0,1)$, which depends $n$ and $x$.

Since $x\geq 0$, we have $1+\theta x\geq 1$.

Furthermore we'll show that $\sum_n\pmat{r\\n} x^n$ converges absolutely: the ratio of terms is $$\left|\frac{\pmat{r\\n+1}x^{n+1}}{\pmat{r\\n}x}\right| = \left|\frac{r-n}{n+1}\right||x| \to |x| < 1$$ so by the ratio test, the series converges absolutely and hence converges, and therefore $\pmat{r\\n}x^n \to 0$ as $n\to\infty$.

Hence $$ |R_n(x)| = \left|\pmat{r\\n} \frac{x^n}{(1+\theta x)^{n-r}}\right| \leq \left|\pmat{r\\n} x^n\right| \to 0$$ so $R_n(x) \to 0$.
