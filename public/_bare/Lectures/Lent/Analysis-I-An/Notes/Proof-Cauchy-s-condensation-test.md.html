Since $a_n$ is decreasing, we have the inequality $$ a_{2^k} \leq a_{2^{k-1} + i} \leq a_{2^{k-1}}, \quad 0\leq i \leq 2^{k-1}. $$

=>: Suppose $\sum a_n$ converges. We have $$ \begin{align*} \sum_{k=1}^K 2^{k-1}a_{2^k} &= \fsum k1K \fsum n{2^{k-1}+1}{2^k} a_{2^k} \\ &\leq \fsum k1K \fsum n{2^{k-1}+1}{2^k} a_n \\ &= \fsum n2{2^K} a_n \leq \fsum n1\infty a_n \end{align*} $$ so $\sum{2^k a_{2^k}}$ is an increasing sequence that is bounded above, so it converges.

<=: Suppose $\sum 2^k a_{2^k}$ converges. $$\begin{align*} \fsum n2{2^K}a_n &= \fsum k1K\fsum n{2^{k-1}+1}{2^k} a_n \\ &\leq \fsum k1K \fsum n{2^{k-1}+1}{2^k} a_{2^{k-1}} \\ &= \fsum k1K 2^{k-1} a_{2^{k-1}} \leq \fsum k1\infty 2^{k-1}a_{2^{k-1}} \end{align*}$$ and for any $N$, pick $K$ that makes $2^K > N$. Then $$ \fsum n2N \leq \fsum n2{2^K} a_n \leq \fsum k1\infty 2^{k-1}a_{2^{k-1}} $$ so the partial sums are bounded and increasing. Hence it converges.
