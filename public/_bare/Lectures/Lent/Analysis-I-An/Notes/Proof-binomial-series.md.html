Proof (binomial series for $x>0$) open_in_new

Clearly $$ f^{(n)}(x) = r(r-1)\ldots(r-n+1)(1+x)^{r-n}. $$

Consider the Cauchy's remainder $$ \begin{align*} R_n &= \frac{(1-\theta)^{n-1}x^n}{(n-1)!} r(r-1)\ldots(r-n+1)(1+\theta x)^{r-n} \\ &= r \Big(\frac{1-\theta}{1+\theta x}\Big)^{n-1}(1+\theta x)^{r-1}\pmat{r-1\\n-1}x^n \end{align*} $$

Note that $1+\theta x>1-\theta$ since $x\in(-1,1)$, so $$ \Big(\frac{1-\theta}{1+\theta x}\Big)^{n-1} < 1. $$ Also we can bound $$ (1+\theta x)^{r-1} \leq \max\set{(1-|x|)^{r-1}, (1+|x|)^{r-1}}. $$ Therefore $$\begin{align*} |R_n| &\leq \ub{r|x|\max\set{(1-|x|)^{r-1}, (1+|x|)^{r-1}}}_\text{independent of $n$} \left|\pmat{r-1\\n-1} x^{n-1}\right|\\ &\to0 \quad \text{as }n\to\infty \end{align*}$$ as shown here.

Thus $R_n\to 0$ as $n\to\infty$ for $x$ fixed in $(-1,1)$.
