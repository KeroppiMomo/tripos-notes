Note that $fg = \frac{1}{4}\left((f+g)^2 - (f-g)^2\right)$ so it suffices to show that $f^2$ is integrable.

Firstly $$ \begin{align*} f(x)^2 - f(y)^2 &= \left(f(x) + f(y)\right)\left(f(x) - f(y)\right) \\ &\leq \left(|f(x)| + |f(y)|\right)\left(f(x) - f(y)\right) \\ &\leq 2\sup_I |f| \left(\sup_I f - \inf_I f\right) \end{align*} $$ and taking supremum over $x$ and $y$ gives $$ \begin{align*} \sup_I f^2 - \inf_I f^2 \leq 2\sup_I|f|\left(\sup_I f - \inf_I f\right). \end{align*} $$

Let $|f(x)| \leq K$ for all $x\in[a,b]$. Then $$ \begin{align*} U(f^2, \mathcal{D}) - L(f^2, \mathcal{D}) &\leq \sum_{j=1}^{n} 2(x_j - x_{j-1}) \sup_{[x_{j-1}, x_j]} |f|\left(\sup_{[x_{j-1}, x_j]} f - \inf_{[x_{j-1}, x_j]} f\right) \\ &\leq 2\sup_{[a,b]} |f| \left(U(f,\mathcal{D}) - L(f,\mathcal{D})\right) \\ &\leq 2K \left(U(f,\mathcal{D}) - L(f,\mathcal{D})\right). \end{align*} $$

Therefore using Cauchy criterion, given $\epsilon>0$, there exists $\mathcal{D}$ such that $$ \begin{align*} & U(f,\mathcal{D}) - L(f,\mathcal{D}) < \frac{\epsilon}{2K} \implies U(f^2,\mathcal{D}) - L(f^2,\mathcal{D}) < \epsilon. \end{align*} $$ Hence $f^2$ is integrable.
