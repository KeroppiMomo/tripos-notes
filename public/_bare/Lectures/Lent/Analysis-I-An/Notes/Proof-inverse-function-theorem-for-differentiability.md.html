$f$ is strictly increasing on $[a,b]$, and from the previous inverse function theorem, $f:[a,b]\to[c,d]$ is a bijection, and $f\inv: [c,d]\to[a,b]$ is continuous and strictly increasing.

Let $y\in(c,d)$ and set $x=f\inv(y)$. For all $h$ such that $y+h\in(c,d)$, let $k=f\inv(y+h) - x$ be such that $$ y+h = f(x+k). $$

Then $$ \frac{f\inv(y+h) - f\inv(y)}{h} = \frac{x+k-x}{f(x+k) - f(x)} = \frac{k}{f(x+k)-f(x)}.$$

Hence as $h\to0$, since $f\inv$ is a continuous function, $k\to 0$, so $$ \frac{f\inv(y+h) - f\inv(y)}{h} \to \frac{1}{f'(x)}. $$

So $$ (f\inv)'(y) = \frac{1}{f'(f\inv(y))}. $$
