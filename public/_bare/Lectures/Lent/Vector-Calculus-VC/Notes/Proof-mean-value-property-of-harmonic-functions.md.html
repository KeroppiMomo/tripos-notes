In spherical polars, $dS = r^2\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi$. Let $$ \begin{align*} \bar\psi(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi r^2} \int_{S_r} \psi(\v x) \, dS. \end{align*} $$ Then $$ \begin{align*} \bar\psi(r) &= \frac{1}{4\pi} \int_{0}^{2\pi} d\phi \int_{0}^{\pi} d\theta\,(\sin\theta) \psi(r,\theta,\phi) \\ \dfd{\bar\psi(R)}{r} &= \frac{1}{4\pi} \int_{0}^{2\pi} d\phi \int_{0}^{\pi} d\theta\,(\sin\theta)\pdfd\psi r \\ &= \frac{1}{4\pi R^2} \int_{S_R} \pdfd\psi r(R) \, dS \\ &= \frac{1}{4\pi R^2}\int_{S_R} \nabla\psi\cdot d\v S \\ &= \frac{1}{4\pi R^2} \int_{\text{Ball}} \nabla^2\psi \, dV = 0. \end{align*} $$ Since $\bar\psi(R)\to\psi(\v a)$ as $R\to 0$, we have $\bar\psi(R) = \psi(\v a)$.
