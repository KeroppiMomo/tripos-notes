Definition (area integral) open_in_new

For a region $D\subset \R^2$, define the area integral of a scalar field $\phi:\R^2\to\R$ over $D$ as $$\int_D \phi(\v x)\, dA \equiv \iint_D \phi(\v x)\, dA = \lim_{n\to0} \sum_n \phi(\v x_n)\, \delta A $$ by dividing $D$ into small area elements and adding up the values of $\phi$.

Fubini's theorem states that any way of splitting $\int dA$ always gives the same answer for well-behaved $\phi$ and $D$.

Definition (Jacobian) open_in_new

For functions $(u(x,y), v(x,y))$, the Jacobian is $$ J(u,v) \equiv \pdfd{(x,y)}{(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix}\partial x/\partial u & \partial x/\partial v \\ \partial y/\partial u & \partial y/\partial v \end{vmatrix}. $$

Proposition (change of variable of area integral) open_in_new

Assume $(x,y)\mapsto (u,v)$ is a smooth and invertible map. The area integral can be written as $$ \int_D dx\,dy\,\phi(x,y) = \int_{D'} du\,dv\,|J(u,v)|\,\phi(u,v) $$ where $D'$ is the image of $D$ and $J(u,v)$ is the Jacobian.

Example (2D polar integral) open_in_new

Polar coordinates are defined by $$ x=\rho\cos\phi, \quad y=\rho\sin\phi $$ with $\rho\in[0,\infty)$ and $\phi\in[0,2\pi)$.

The Jacobian is $$ J = \begin{vmatrix} \cos\phi & -\rho\sin\phi \\ \sin\phi & \rho\cos\phi \end{vmatrix} = \rho $$ so the area element is $dA = \rho\, d\rho\, d\phi$.

Same thing for a volume integral.

Example (spherical polar integral) open_in_new

Spherical polar coordinates are defined as $$\begin{align*} x &= r\sin\theta\cos\phi & & & r &\in [0,\infty) \\ y &= r\sin\theta\sin\phi & &\text{with} & \theta &\in [0,\pi] \\ z &= r\cos\theta & & & \phi &\in [0,2\pi). \end{align*}$$

The Jacobian is $$ J = \begin{vmatrix}\sin\theta\cos\phi & r\cos\theta\cos\phi & -r\sin\theta\sin\phi \\ \sin\theta\sin\phi & r\cos\theta\sin\phi & r\sin\theta\cos\phi \\ \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta & 0 \end{vmatrix} = r^2 \sin\theta. $$

Example (cylindrical polar integral) open_in_new

Cylindrical polar coordinates are given by $$\begin{align*} x &= \rho \cos \phi & & & \rho&\in[0,\infty) \\ y &= \rho \sin \phi & &\text{with} & \phi&\in[0, 2\pi) \\ z &= z &&& z&\in(-\infty, \infty). \end{align*}$$

The Jacobian is $$ J = \rho. $$

Definition (surface in R3) open_in_new

Informally, there are two ways to define a surface in $\R^3$:

  • as a level set of a function $F(x,y,z) = 0;$

  • or as a parametrised surface $\v x: \R^2 \to \R^3.$

Normal vector of a surface open_in_new

The normal vector $\v n$ of a surface points perpendicular away from the surface.

  • For the surface $F(\v x) = 0$, $\v n\parallel \nabla F$ (Derivation). Define $$ \v n = \pm \frac1{|\nabla F|} \nabla F. $$

  • For a parametrised surface $\v x(u, v)$, $$ \v n \parallel \pdvd xu \times \pdvd xv. $$

Definition (regular surface) open_in_new

If the normal vector $\v n\neq \v 0$ at all points on the surface, then the surface is regular.

Definition (bounded surface) open_in_new

A surface is bounded if it does not stretch to infinity. Otherwise it is unbounded.

Definition (boundary, closed surface) open_in_new

The boundary of a surface $S$ is a closed curve denoted $\partial S$.

A bounded surface with no boundary is closed.

Definition (orientable surface) open_in_new

If there is a consistent choice of the normal vector which varies smoothly over a surface, then the surface is orientable.

Definition (surface integral of a scalar field) open_in_new

For a surface $S$ parametrised by $\v x(u, v)$ and a scalar field $\phi: \R^3\to\R$, the surface integral of $\phi$ over $S$ is $$ \int_S \phi(\v x)\, dS = \int_D \bigg|\pdvd x u \times \pdvd x v\bigg|\ \phi(\v x(u,v))\, du\, dv $$ where $D$ is the region in the $(u,v)$ plane.

Definition (surface integral of a vector field) open_in_new

For a surface $S$ parametrised by $\v x(u, v)$ and a vector field $\v F: \R^3\to\R^3$, the surface integral of $\v F$ over $S$ is $$ \int_S \v F(\v x)\cdot d\v S = \int_D \Big(\pdvd xu\times\pdvd xv\Big)\cdot \v F\big(\v x(u,v)\big) \,du\,dv$$ where $d\v S = \v n\, dS$ is the area element. The integral is also called the flux of $\v F$ through the surface $S$.

Think of as rate of fluid flowing through the surface.

  • The integral for a scalar field does not depend on orientation, but it does for a vector field.

  • The integral is reparametrisation invariant (up to a sign for vector field). (Proof)
