Gauss' law open_in_new

We have Newton's force and Coulomb's force $$ \v F(\v x) = m\v g(\v x) + q\v E(\v x). $$ where the gravitational field and electric field can be defined in three equivalent ways: $$ \text{inverse square law} \iff \text{Gauss' law in integrated form} \iff \text{Gauss' law in differential form} $$ $$ \begin{align*} \v g(r) &= -\frac{GM}{r^2}\vhat r &&\iff& \int_S \v g\cdot d\v S &= -4\pi GM &&\iff& \nabla\cdot\v g &= -4\pi G\rho(\v x), \\ \v E(r) &= -\frac{Q}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r^2}\vhat r &&\iff& \int_S \v E\cdot d\v S &= \frac{Q}{\epsilon_0} &&\iff& \nabla\cdot\v E &= \frac{\rho(\v x)}{\epsilon_0}. \\ \end{align*}$$ Proof

Poisson equation and Laplace equation open_in_new

$$\begin{align*} \text{Poisson equation}& & \nabla^2\phi &= f(\v x). \\ \text{Laplace equation}& & \nabla^2\phi &= 0. \end{align*}$$ In static conditions, gravitational field $\v g$ and electric field $\v E$ are conservative, so the potentials of the fields satisfy the Poisson equation.

Solutions to the Laplace equation $\nabla^2\psi=0$ are called harmonic functions.

  • $\nabla^2$ is linear, so sum of solutions is also a solution. Solutions to Laplace equation are complementary solutions to Poisson.

  • If $\psi(r)$ is a solution, so is any derviative of $\psi$, e.g. $\v d\cdot\nabla\psi$.

Isotropic solutions of Laplace equation open_in_new

  • With spherical symmetry $\psi(\v x) = \psi(r)$, the solution is $$ \begin{align*} \psi(r) = \frac{A}{r} + B. \end{align*} $$

  • With cylindrical polar $\psi(\v x) = \psi(\rho)$, the solution is $$ \begin{align*} \psi(\rho) = A \log \rho + B. \end{align*} $$

(Derivation) Example of solving isotropic Poisson equation with boundary conditions

Common boundary conditions: in some region $V\subset\R^3$,

  • Dirichlet condition: fix $\psi(\v x)$ on $\partial V$;

  • Newmann condition: fix $\v n\cdot\nabla \psi(\v x)$ on $\partial V$, where $\v n$ is the outward pointing normal of $\partial V$.

Proposition (Green's identities) open_in_new

$$ \begin{align*} \int_{V} \phi\nabla^2\psi \, dV = \int_{\partial V} \phi\nabla\psi \cdot d\v S - \int_{V} \nabla\phi\cdot\nabla\psi \, dV \end{align*} $$ (Derivation) And swapping the roles of $\phi,\psi$ and subtracting gives $$ \begin{align*} \int_{V} (\phi\nabla^2\psi-\psi\nabla^2\phi) \, dV = \int_{\partial V} (\phi\nabla\psi - \psi\nabla\phi) \cdot d\v S. \end{align*} $$

Proposition (unique solution to Poisson equation) open_in_new

If there is a solution to the Poisson equation on a region $V$ with either Dirichlet or Newmann boundary conditions specified on each $\partial V$, then it is unique up to a constant.

In particular if Dirichlet condition is specified anywhere, then the solution is unique.

Proof

In general we can extend this to unbounded region with specified asymptotic boundary conditions.

Proposition (existence of solution of Poisson equation under Newmann condition) open_in_new

For Poisson equation $\nabla^2\psi = -\rho(\v x)$ with Newmann condition $\v n\cdot\nabla\psi = g(\v x)$ on $\partial V$, $$ \begin{align*} -\int_{V} \rho(\v x) \, dV = \int_{\partial V} g(\v x) \, dS. \end{align*} $$

Proof

Proposition (mean value property of harmonic functions) open_in_new

If $\psi$ is harmonic in a region $V$ that includes a ball with boundary $S_R: |\v x-\v a|=R$, then the value of $\psi(\v a)$ is the average of $\psi$ on the sphere: $$ \begin{align*} \psi(\v a) = \frac{1}{4\pi R^2} \int_{S_R} \psi(\v x) \, dS. \end{align*} $$

Proof

Corollary (no extreme value of harmonic functions inside) open_in_new

A harmonic function can have neither a maximum or minimum in the interior of $V$.

Proof

Theorem (integral solutions of Poisson equation) open_in_new

$\nabla^2\psi = -\rho(\v x)$ has integral solution $$ \begin{align*} \psi(\v x) = \frac{1}{4\pi}\int_{V'} \frac{\rho(\v x')}{|\v x-\v x'|} \, dV'. \end{align*} $$
