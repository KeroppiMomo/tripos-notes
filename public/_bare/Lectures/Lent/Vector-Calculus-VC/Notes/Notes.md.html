Curves open_in_new

Definition (parametrised curve) open_in_new

For a continuous function $$ f: \R \to \R^n, $$ the image of $f$ is called a parametrised curve with $t$ the parameter.

The choice of parametrisation is not unique, and plotting the curve loses information about the parameter $t$.

Differentiating a curve open_in_new

Definition (differentiation of vector function) open_in_new

A function $\v x(t)$ is differentiable at $t$ if $$\v x(t+\delta t) - \v x(t) = \dv x(t) \delta t + O(\delta t^2). $$ Then the derivative is $$ \dfd{\v x}t \equiv \dv x = \lim_{\delta t\to0}\frac{\delta\v x}{\delta t}$$ where $\delta\v x(t) = \v x(t+\delta t) - \v x(t)$.

Definition (smooth curve) open_in_new

If $f: \R \to \R^n$ is differentiable everywhere, the curve is said to be smooth.

Properties of differentiation of vector function open_in_new

  • In any bases independent of $t$, we just differentiate vectors component-wise: $$ \v x(t) = x^i(t) \v e_i \implies \dv x(t) = \dot x^i(t) \v e_i. $$

For real function $f(t)$ and vector functions $\v g(t), \v h(t)$, these can be seen by applying the product rules to the components:

  • $\dd t(f\v g) = \dot f\v g + f\dv g$;

  • $\dd t (\v g\cdot\v h) = \dv g\cdot\v h + \v g\cdot\dv h$;

  • $\dd t (\v g\times\v h) = \dv g\times \v h + \v g\times\dv h$.

Tangent vector open_in_new

Definition (tangent vector) open_in_new

The derivative $\dv x(t)$ is called a tangent vector to the curve at $t$.

The direction of $\dv x(t)$ is independent of the choice of parametrisation up to a sign, but the magnitude is not.

Definition (regular parametrisation) open_in_new

A parametrisation is called regular if $\dv x(t) \neq \v 0$ for any $t$.

We assume regularity from now on.

Arc length open_in_new

Definition (arc length of a curve) open_in_new

For some starting point $t_0$, the arc length $s(t)$ is the (signed) distance from $t_0$ to $t$, $$ s(t) = \int_{t_0}^t |\dv x(t')|\,dt'. $$

  • The arc length between two points on a curve is independent of the choice of parametrisation. (Proof)

  • Using $s$ to parametrise the curve i.e. $\v x(s)$, the tangent vector has unit norm: $$ \bigg|\dvd xs\bigg| = \frac{|d\v x/dt|}{ds/dt} = 1. $$

Curvature open_in_new

Definition (curvature of a curve) open_in_new

Let $\v x(s)$ be a parametrisation by the arc length. The curvature is $$ \kappa(s) = \bigg|\dvd[2]xs\bigg| = \bigg|\dvd ts\bigg| $$ where $\v t = d\v x/ds$ is the unit tangent vector.

If the curve is locally a circle of radius $R$, the curvature at that point is $1/R$.

Definition (principal normal) open_in_new

The principal normal at a point $\v x(s)$ is $$ \v n = \frac1\kappa\dvd[2]xs = \frac1\kappa\dvd ts. $$ $1/\kappa$ ensures this is a unit vector.

Proposition (principal normal perpendicular to tangent) open_in_new

If curvature $\kappa\neq 0$, then $\v n\cdot\v t=0$.

Proof

Defintion (osculating plane) open_in_new

The plane spanned by $\v t$ and $\v n$ is called the osculating plane.

  • In $\R^3$, since $\v t\cdot\dvd ts=0$, we can compute the curvature as $$ \kappa = \bigg|\v t\times\dvd ts\bigg| $$

Binormal open_in_new

Definition (binormal) open_in_new

For curves in $\R^3$, define the binormal as $$ \v b = \v t\times\v n. $$

When $\kappa(s)\neq 0$, the vectors $\v t, \v n, \v b$ form an orthonormal basis for each $s$.

Definition (torsion) open_in_new

For a curve in $\R^3$, the torsion $\tau(s)$ is defined as $$ \dvd bs = -\tau\v n. $$

Derivation that change in b is parallel to n

The torsion measure how much the curve twists out of the plane.

Curvature and torsion specify a curve open_in_new

The tangent vectors and binormal vectors satisfy two first order differential equations (so in total six ODEs accounting the components): $$\begin{cases} \dvd ts = \kappa\v n = \kappa\v b\times\v t \\ \dvd bs = -\tau\v n = \tau\v t\times\v b \end{cases}$$ So for fixed $\kappa(s)$ and $\tau(s)$, there is a unique solution given some initial $\v b(0)$ and $\v t(0)$.

Hence the curvature and torsion function specifies a curve up to rotation and translation.

Line integral open_in_new

Manipulating curves open_in_new

  • The choice of direction along $C$ is called an orientation.

  • The curve $-C$ is the curve $C$ with the opposite orientation.

  • For curves $C_1, C_2$, we can piece together the curves to get $C=C_1 + C_2$.

Definition (line integral of a scalar field) open_in_new

For a scalar field $\phi: \R^n\to\R$, the line integral of $\phi(\v x)$ along a curve $C: \v x(t)$ from $\v x(t_a) = \v a$ to $\v x(t_b) = \v b$ where $t_a < t_b$ is $$ \int_C\phi\,ds = \int_{t_a}^{t_b} \phi\big(\v x(t)\big)\,|\dv x|\,dt. $$

In particular for parametrisation using arc length, $$\int_C\phi\, ds = \int_{s_a}^{s_b} \phi\big(\v x(s)\big)\,ds. $$

Definition (line integral of a vector field) open_in_new

For a vector field $\v F: \R^n \to \R^n$, the line integral of $\v F(\v x)$ along the curve $C:\v x(t)$ from $\v x(t_a) = \v a$ to $\v x(t_b) = \v b$ is $$ \int_C \v F\cdot d\v x = \int_{t_a}^{t_b} \v F\big(\v x(t)\big)\cdot \dv x(t)\, dt. $$

When $C$ is a closed path, i.e. $\v a = \v b$, the integral is called the circulation of $\v F$ around $C$, denoted $$ \oint_C \v F\cdot d\v x. $$

Properties of line integrals open_in_new

  • The line integral of a vector field is the integral of $\v F$ tangent to the curve.

  • Both line integrals are invariant under reparametrisation.

  • The line integral of a scalar field does not depend on orientation since we impose $t_a<t_b$: $$\int_{-C} \phi\,ds = \int_C \phi\,ds,$$ but the line integral of a vector field does: $$\int_{-C} \v F\cdot d\v x = -\int_C \v F\cdot d\v x.$$

  • For a piecewise smooth curve $C_1 + C_2$, $$\int_{C_1+C_2}\v F\cdot d\v x = \int_{C_1}\v F\cdot d\v x + \int_{C_2}\v F\cdot d\v x.$$

Conservative vector field open_in_new

Definition (partial differentiation) open_in_new

For a scalar field $\phi: \R^n \to \R$, the partial derivatives are defined to be $$ \pdfd\phi{x^1}(\v x) = \lim_{\epsilon\to0}\frac1\epsilon\big(\phi(x^1 + \epsilon, x^2, x^3, \ldots, x^n) - \phi(x^1, x^2, \ldots, x^n)\big)$$ and similarly for $\partial\phi/\partial x^i$.

Definition (gradient) open_in_new

For a scalar field $\phi: \R^n \to \R$, let $\set{\v e_i}$ be an orthonormal basis of $\R^n$. The gradient of $\phi$ is a vector field defined as $$ \v\nabla\phi = \pdfd\phi{x^i} \v e_i. $$

Definition (conservative vector field) open_in_new

A vector field $\v F$ is called conservative if it can be written as $$ \v F = \v\nabla \phi $$ for some scalar field $\phi$ called the potential.

Theorem (conservative field and line integral) open_in_new

The following are equivalent: for a vector field $\v F: \R^n \to \R^n$,

  1. $\oint_C \v F\cdot d\v x = 0$ for all closed curve $C$;

  2. $\int_C \v F\cdot d\v x$ only depends the endpoints of $C$.

  3. $\v F$ is conservative;

Proof

Condition for a conservative vector field open_in_new

For a vector field $\v F$, a necessary condition is $$ \pdfd{F_i}{x^j} = \frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial x^i \partial x^j} = \pdfd{F_j}{x^i}.$$ This turns out to be a sufficient condition for well-behaved functions.

Definition (exact differential) open_in_new

Given a function $\phi(\v x)$, its exact differential is $$ d\phi = \pdfd \phi{x^i} dx^i = \nabla\phi \cdot d\v x.$$

For a vector field $\v F$, $\v F\cdot d\v x$ is an exact differential form if it can be written as $\v F\cdot d\v x=d\phi$. This means $\v F=\nabla\phi$.

If a vector field has singular points, even if it is conservative, $\oint_C \v F\cdot d\v x$ may not be zero for a curve $C$ around the singular point.

Multiple integrals open_in_new

Definition (area integral) open_in_new

For a region $D\subset \R^2$, define the area integral of a scalar field $\phi:\R^2\to\R$ over $D$ as $$\int_D \phi(\v x)\, dA \equiv \iint_D \phi(\v x)\, dA = \lim_{n\to0} \sum_n \phi(\v x_n)\, \delta A $$ by dividing $D$ into small area elements and adding up the values of $\phi$.

Fubini's theorem states that any way of splitting $\int dA$ always gives the same answer for well-behaved $\phi$ and $D$.

Definition (Jacobian) open_in_new

For functions $(u(x,y), v(x,y))$, the Jacobian is $$ J(u,v) \equiv \pdfd{(x,y)}{(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix}\partial x/\partial u & \partial x/\partial v \\ \partial y/\partial u & \partial y/\partial v \end{vmatrix}. $$

Proposition (change of variable of area integral) open_in_new

Assume $(x,y)\mapsto (u,v)$ is a smooth and invertible map. The area integral can be written as $$ \int_D dx\,dy\,\phi(x,y) = \int_{D'} du\,dv\,|J(u,v)|\,\phi(u,v) $$ where $D'$ is the image of $D$ and $J(u,v)$ is the Jacobian.

Example (2D polar integral) open_in_new

Polar coordinates are defined by $$ x=\rho\cos\phi, \quad y=\rho\sin\phi $$ with $\rho\in[0,\infty)$ and $\phi\in[0,2\pi)$.

The Jacobian is $$ J = \begin{vmatrix} \cos\phi & -\rho\sin\phi \\ \sin\phi & \rho\cos\phi \end{vmatrix} = \rho $$ so the area element is $dA = \rho\, d\rho\, d\phi$.

Same thing for a volume integral.

Example (spherical polar integral) open_in_new

Spherical polar coordinates are defined as $$\begin{align*} x &= r\sin\theta\cos\phi & & & r &\in [0,\infty) \\ y &= r\sin\theta\sin\phi & &\text{with} & \theta &\in [0,\pi] \\ z &= r\cos\theta & & & \phi &\in [0,2\pi). \end{align*}$$

The Jacobian is $$ J = \begin{vmatrix}\sin\theta\cos\phi & r\cos\theta\cos\phi & -r\sin\theta\sin\phi \\ \sin\theta\sin\phi & r\cos\theta\sin\phi & r\sin\theta\cos\phi \\ \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta & 0 \end{vmatrix} = r^2 \sin\theta. $$

Example (cylindrical polar integral) open_in_new

Cylindrical polar coordinates are given by $$\begin{align*} x &= \rho \cos \phi & & & \rho&\in[0,\infty) \\ y &= \rho \sin \phi & &\text{with} & \phi&\in[0, 2\pi) \\ z &= z &&& z&\in(-\infty, \infty). \end{align*}$$

The Jacobian is $$ J = \rho. $$

Surface integrals open_in_new

Surface in R3 open_in_new

Definition (surface in R3) open_in_new

Informally, there are two ways to define a surface in $\R^3$:

  • as a level set of a function $F(x,y,z) = 0;$

  • or as a parametrised surface $\v x: \R^2 \to \R^3.$

Normal vector of a surface open_in_new

The normal vector $\v n$ of a surface points perpendicular away from the surface.

  • For the surface $F(\v x) = 0$, $\v n\parallel \nabla F$ (Derivation). Define $$ \v n = \pm \frac1{|\nabla F|} \nabla F. $$

  • For a parametrised surface $\v x(u, v)$, $$ \v n \parallel \pdvd xu \times \pdvd xv. $$

Definition (regular surface) open_in_new

If the normal vector $\v n\neq \v 0$ at all points on the surface, then the surface is regular.

Definition (bounded surface) open_in_new

A surface is bounded if it does not stretch to infinity. Otherwise it is unbounded.

Definition (boundary, closed surface) open_in_new

The boundary of a surface $S$ is a closed curve denoted $\partial S$.

A bounded surface with no boundary is closed.

Definition (orientable surface) open_in_new

If there is a consistent choice of the normal vector which varies smoothly over a surface, then the surface is orientable.

Definition (surface integral of a scalar field) open_in_new

For a surface $S$ parametrised by $\v x(u, v)$ and a scalar field $\phi: \R^3\to\R$, the surface integral of $\phi$ over $S$ is $$ \int_S \phi(\v x)\, dS = \int_D \bigg|\pdvd x u \times \pdvd x v\bigg|\ \phi(\v x(u,v))\, du\, dv $$ where $D$ is the region in the $(u,v)$ plane.

Definition (surface integral of a vector field) open_in_new

For a surface $S$ parametrised by $\v x(u, v)$ and a vector field $\v F: \R^3\to\R^3$, the surface integral of $\v F$ over $S$ is $$ \int_S \v F(\v x)\cdot d\v S = \int_D \Big(\pdvd xu\times\pdvd xv\Big)\cdot \v F\big(\v x(u,v)\big) \,du\,dv$$ where $d\v S = \v n\, dS$ is the area element. The integral is also called the flux of $\v F$ through the surface $S$.

Think of as rate of fluid flowing through the surface.

  • The integral for a scalar field does not depend on orientation, but it does for a vector field.

  • The integral is reparametrisation invariant (up to a sign for vector field). (Proof)

See lecture notes for a (non-examinable) discussion on the Gauss-Bonnet Theorem.

Grad, div, curl open_in_new

Definition (nabla) open_in_new

Define the gradient operator $$ \nabla = \v e_i \pdd{x^i}. $$

Definition (gradient without coordinates) open_in_new

Alternatively to here, we can define the gradient of a scalar field $\phi: \R^n \to \R$ by $$ \phi(\v x+\v h) = \phi(\v x) + \v h\cdot\nabla\phi + O(|\v h|^2). $$

Proposition (scalar field following a curve) open_in_new

Let $\v x(t): \R \to \R^n$ be a curve in $\R^n$ and $\phi: \R^n \to \R$ be a scalar field. Then $$ \dvd{\phi\big(\v x(t)\big)}t = \nabla\phi\cdot\dvd xt.$$ Proof

Definition (divergence) open_in_new

For a vector field $\v F: \R^n\to\R^n$, the divergence of $\v F$ is a scalar field $$ \nabla\cdot\v F = \pdfd{F_i}{x^i}. $$

E.g. in $\R^3$, $$\nabla\cdot\v F = \pdfd{F_x}x + \pdfd{F_y}y + \pdfd{F_z}z. $$ Think as the net flow of $\v F$ out of ($\nabla\cdot\v F>0$) or into ($\nabla\cdot\v F<0$) the point $\v x$.

Definition (curl) open_in_new

For a vector field $\v F: \R^3\to\R^3$, the curl of $\v F$ is $$ \nabla \times \v F = \epsilon_{ijk} \pdfd{F_j}{x_i} \v e_k.$$

Think as the rotation of $\v F$ using the right-hand rule.

Definition (irrotational, solenoidal) open_in_new

A vector field $\v F: \R^3\to\R^3$ is

  • irrotational if $\nabla\times\v F=\v 0$;

  • divergence-free/solenoidal if $\nabla\cdot\v F=0$.

Definition (Laplacian) open_in_new

The Laplacian is a second-order differential operator: $$ \nabla^2 = \nabla\cdot\nabla = \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^i\partial x^i}. $$

E.g. in $\R^3$, $$ \nabla^2 = \pdd[2] x + \pdd[2]y + \pdd[2]z.$$ $\nabla^2\phi$ is a scalar field, and $\nabla^2\v F$ is a vector field, acting component-wise.

Properties:

  • Rotational symmetry: let $\v r = r\vhat r$. $$ \begin{align*} \nabla r^p &= pr^{p-1} \vhat r, \\ \nabla\cdot\v r &= n &\text{in }\R^n, \\ \nabla\times\v r &= \v 0. \end{align*} $$ (Derivation)

    For constant $\alpha$, scalar fields $\phi(\v x), \psi(\v x)$ and vectors fields $\v F(\v x), \v G(\v x)$,

  • $\nabla$ is linear operator: $$ \begin{align*} \nabla(\alpha\phi + \psi) &= \alpha \nabla \phi + \nabla\psi \\ \nabla\cdot(\alpha\phi + \psi) &= \alpha \nabla\cdot \phi + \nabla\cdot\psi \\ \nabla\times(\alpha\phi + \psi) &= \alpha \nabla\times \phi + \nabla\times\psi \\ \end{align*} $$

  • Leibniz properties: $$\begin{align*} \nabla(\phi\psi) &= \psi\nabla\phi+\phi\nabla\psi \\ \nabla\cdot(\phi\v F) &= (\nabla\phi)\cdot\v F + \phi(\nabla\cdot \v F) \\ \nabla\times(\phi\v F) &= (\nabla\phi)\times\v F + \phi(\nabla\times\v F) \end{align*}$$ and more: $$\begin{align*} \nabla\cdot(\v F\times\v G) &= (\nabla\times\v F)\cdot\v G-\v F\cdot(\nabla\times\v G) \\ \nabla(\v F\cdot\v G) &= \v F\times(\nabla\times\v G) + \v G\times(\nabla\times F) + (\v F\cdot\nabla)\v G + (\v G\cdot\nabla)\v F \\ \nabla\times(\v F\times\v G) &= (\nabla\cdot\v G)\v F - (\nabla\cdot \v F)\v G + (\v G\cdot\nabla)\v F - (\v F\cdot\nabla)\v G \end{align*}$$ (Derivation)

Orthogonal curvilinear coordinates open_in_new

Definition (orthogonal curvilinear coordinates) open_in_new

For coordinates $\v x(u,v,w)$, it is said to be orthogonal curvilinear if $\partial\v x/\partial u$, $\partial\v x/\partial v$, $\partial\v x/\partial w$ are mutually orthogonal.

We can introduce normalised tangent vectors $$ \pdvd xu = h_u \v e_u, \quad \pdvd xv = h_v \v e_v, \quad \pdvd xw = h_w \v e_w $$ where $h_u, h_v, h_w>0$, $\v e_u, \v e_v, \v e_w$ orthonormal and by reordering the coordinates $\v e_u\times\v e_v = \v e_w$.

Then we have length element $$ d\v x = h_u \v e_u\,du + h_v \v e_v\,dv + h_w\v e_w\,dw. $$

Lemma (nabla in orthogonal curvilinear coordinates) open_in_new

In orthogonal curvilinear coordinates $\v x(u,v,w)$, $$ \nabla = \frac1{h_u}\v e_u \pdd u + \frac1{h_v}\v e_v\pdd v + \frac1{h_w}\v e_w \pdd w $$ and for scalar field $f: \R^3\to\R$ and vector field $\v F:\R^3\to\R^3$, $$\begin{align*} \nabla f &= \frac1{h_u} \pdfd fu \v e_u + \frac1{h_v}\pdfd fv\v e_v + \frac1{h_w}\pdfd fw \v e_w, \\ \nabla\cdot\v F &= \frac{1}{h_u h_v h_w} \bigg(\pdd u(h_v h_w F_u) + \pdd v(h_u h_w F_v) + \pdd w (h_uh_v F_w)\bigg), \\ \nabla\times\v F &= \frac1{h_uh_v h_w}\begin{vmatrix}h_u\v e_u & h_v\v e_v & h_w\v e_w \\ \pdd u & \pdd v & \pdd w \\ h_uF_u & h_v F_v & h_w F_w \end{vmatrix}. \end{align*}$$

Proof

Example (cylindrical polar coordinates) open_in_new

Cylindrical polar coordinates are given by $$\begin{align*} x &= \rho \cos \phi & & & \rho&\in[0,\infty) \\ y &= \rho \sin \phi & &\text{with} & \phi&\in[0, 2\pi) \\ z &= z &&& z&\in(-\infty, \infty). \end{align*}$$ The inverse map is $$ \begin{align*} \rho &= \sqrt{x^2+y^2}, \\ \tan\phi &= \frac yx. \end{align*} $$

The orthonormal vectors are $$ \begin{align*} \v e_\rho \equiv \vhat\rho &= (\cos\phi,\sin\phi,0), & h_\rho &= 1, \\ \v e_\phi \equiv \vhat\phi &= (-\sin\phi, \cos\phi,0), & h_\phi &= \rho, \\ \v e_z \equiv\vhat z &= (0,0,1), &h_z &= 1. \end{align*} $$

So the Jacobian is $$ \pdfd{(x,y,z)}{(\rho,\phi,z)} = h_\rho h_\phi h_z = \rho. $$ The derivative stuff are $$\begin{align*} \nabla &= \vhat\rho \pdd \rho + \frac1\rho\vhat\phi\pdd\phi + \vhat z\pdd z, \\ \nabla f &= \vhat\rho \pdfd f \rho + \frac1\rho\vhat\phi\pdfd f\phi + \vhat z\pdfd fz, \\ \nabla\cdot\v F &= \frac1\rho\pdfd{(\rho F_\rho)}\rho + \frac1\rho\pdfd{F_\phi}\phi + \pdfd{F_z}z, \\ \nabla^2 f &= \frac1\rho\pdd\rho\bigg(\rho\pdfd f\rho\bigg) + \frac1{\rho^2}\pdfd[2]f\phi + \pdfd[2]fz. \end{align*}$$

Example (spherical polar coordinates) open_in_new

Spherical polar coordinates are defined as $$\begin{align*} x &= r\sin\theta\cos\phi & & & r &\in [0,\infty) \\ y &= r\sin\theta\sin\phi & &\text{with} & \theta &\in [0,\pi] \\ z &= r\cos\theta & & & \phi &\in [0,2\pi). \end{align*}$$ The inverse map is $$\begin{align*} r &= \sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}, \\ \tan\theta &= \frac{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}z, \\ \tan\phi &= \frac yx. \end{align*}$$

The tangent vectors are $$\begin{align*} \v e_r \equiv \vhat r &= (\sin\theta\cos\phi,\sin\theta\sin\phi,\cos\theta), & h_r &= 1, \\ \v e_\theta \equiv \vhat\theta &= (\cos\theta\cos\phi, \cos\theta\sin\phi, -\sin\theta), &h_\theta &= r, \\ \v e_\phi \equiv \vhat \phi &= (-\sin\phi, \cos\phi, 0), & h_\phi &= r\sin\theta. \end{align*}$$

The derivative stuff are $$\begin{align*} \nabla &= \vhat r \pdd r +\frac1r\vhat\theta \pdd\theta + \frac1{r\sin\theta}\vhat \phi\pdd \phi, \\ \nabla f &= \vhat r \pdfd fr +\frac1r\vhat\theta \pdfd f\theta + \frac1{r\sin\theta}\vhat \phi\pdfd f\phi, \\ \nabla\cdot\v F &= \frac1{r^2} \pdfd{(r^2 F_r)}r + \frac1{r\sin\theta} \pdfd{(\sin\theta F_\theta)}\theta + \frac1{r\sin\theta}\pdfd{F_\phi}{\phi} \\ \nabla^2 f &= \frac1{r^2} \pdd r\bigg(r^2\pdfd fr\bigg) + \frac1{r^2\sin\theta}\pdd\theta\bigg(\sin\theta\pdfd f\theta\bigg) + \frac1{r^2\sin^2\theta} \pdfd[2]f\phi. \end{align*}$$

Continuity equation open_in_new

Suppose a physical quantity is conserved locally, which means that if the total quantity inside some region $V$ decreases at some rate, then the flux out of the region should be at that rate.

More specifically, if $\rho(\v x,t)$ is the density and $\v J(\v x,t)$ is the current density, $$ \pdd t \int_V \rho\,dV = -\int_{\partial V}\v J\cdot d\v S. $$ Using the divergence theorem, we obtain the continuity equation $$ \pdfd\rho t + \nabla\cdot\v J = 0. $$

Lemma (Green's theorem) open_in_new

Let $P(x,y), Q(x,y)$ be smooth functions on $\R^2$. Then $$ \int_A \Big(\pdfd Qx - \pdfd Py\Big) \, dA = \oint_C P\,dx + Q\,dy $$ where $C=\partial A$ going anti-clockwise.

Proof

If $C=\partial A$ has disconnected components, then the inner boundary should go clockwise.

Theorem (Stokes' theorem) open_in_new

Gauss' law open_in_new

We have Newton's force and Coulomb's force $$ \v F(\v x) = m\v g(\v x) + q\v E(\v x). $$ where the gravitational field and electric field can be defined in three equivalent ways: $$ \text{inverse square law} \iff \text{Gauss' law in integrated form} \iff \text{Gauss' law in differential form} $$ $$ \begin{align*} \v g(r) &= -\frac{GM}{r^2}\vhat r &&\iff& \int_S \v g\cdot d\v S &= -4\pi GM &&\iff& \nabla\cdot\v g &= -4\pi G\rho(\v x), \\ \v E(r) &= -\frac{Q}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r^2}\vhat r &&\iff& \int_S \v E\cdot d\v S &= \frac{Q}{\epsilon_0} &&\iff& \nabla\cdot\v E &= \frac{\rho(\v x)}{\epsilon_0}. \\ \end{align*}$$ Proof

Poisson equation and Laplace equation open_in_new

$$\begin{align*} \text{Poisson equation}& & \nabla^2\phi &= f(\v x). \\ \text{Laplace equation}& & \nabla^2\phi &= 0. \end{align*}$$ In static conditions, gravitational field $\v g$ and electric field $\v E$ are conservative, so the potentials of the fields satisfy the Poisson equation.

Solutions to the Laplace equation $\nabla^2\psi=0$ are called harmonic functions.

  • $\nabla^2$ is linear, so sum of solutions is also a solution. Solutions to Laplace equation are complementary solutions to Poisson.

  • If $\psi(r)$ is a solution, so is any derviative of $\psi$, e.g. $\v d\cdot\nabla\psi$.

Isotropic solutions of Laplace equation open_in_new

  • With spherical symmetry $\psi(\v x) = \psi(r)$, the solution is $$ \begin{align*} \psi(r) = \frac{A}{r} + B. \end{align*} $$

  • With cylindrical polar $\psi(\v x) = \psi(\rho)$, the solution is $$ \begin{align*} \psi(\rho) = A \log \rho + B. \end{align*} $$

(Derivation) Example of solving isotropic Poisson equation with boundary conditions

Common boundary conditions: in some region $V\subset\R^3$,

  • Dirichlet condition: fix $\psi(\v x)$ on $\partial V$;

  • Newmann condition: fix $\v n\cdot\nabla(\v x)$ on $\partial V$, where $\v n$ is the outward pointing normal of $\partial V$.

Proposition (Green's identities) open_in_new

$$ \begin{align*} \int_{V} \phi\nabla^2\psi \, dV = \int_{\partial V} \phi\nabla\psi \cdot d\v S - \int_{V} \nabla\phi\cdot\nabla\psi \, dV \end{align*} $$ (Derivation) And swapping the roles of $\phi,\psi$ and subtracting gives $$ \begin{align*} \int_{V} (\phi\nabla^2\psi-\psi\nabla^2\phi) \, dV = \int_{\partial V} (\phi\nabla\psi - \psi\nabla\phi) \cdot d\v S. \end{align*} $$

Proposition (unique solution to Poisson equation) open_in_new

If there is a solution to the Poisson equation on a region $V$ with either Dirichlet or Newmann boundary conditions specified on each $\partial V$, then it is unique up to a constant.

In particular if Dirichlet condition is specified anywhere, then the solution is unique.

Proof

In general we can extend this to unbounded region with specified asymptotic boundary conditions.

For Poisson equation $\nabla^2\psi = -\rho(\v x)$ with Newmann condition $\v n\cdot\nabla\psi = g(\v x)$ on $\partial V$, $$ \begin{align*} -\int_{V} \rho(\v x) \, dV = \int_{\partial V} g(\v x) \, dS. \end{align*} $$

Proof

Proposition (mean value property of harmonic functions) open_in_new

If $\psi$ is harmonic in a region $V$ that includes a ball with boundary $S_R: |\v x-\v a|=R$, then the value of $\psi(\v a)$ is the average of $\psi$ on the sphere: $$ \begin{align*} \psi(\v a) = \frac{1}{4\pi R^2} \int_{S_R} \psi(\v x) \, dS. \end{align*} $$

Proof

Definition (tensor) open_in_new

Equivalent views:

  • A tensor $T$ of rank $p$ (or a $p$-tensor) in $\R^n$ is an array of numbers $T_{i_1 i_2 \ldots i_p}$ where $i_k = 1,2,\ldots,n$, such that under a change of orthonormal basis from $\set{\v e_i}$ to $\set{\v e_i'}$ where $\v e_i' = R_{ij} \v e_j$, the components of the tensors transform as $$ \begin{align*} T_{i_1 i_2 \ldots i_p}' = R_{i_1 j_1} R_{i_2 j_2} \ldots R_{i_p j_p} T_{j_1 j_2 \ldots j_p}. \end{align*} $$

  • A $p$-tensor in $R^n$ is a multi-linear map $$ \begin{align*} T: \ub{\R^n \times \ldots \times\R^n}_{p} \to \R \end{align*} $$ such that in a given basis $\set{\v e_i}$, $$ \begin{align*} T(\v a, \v b, \ldots, \v c) = T_{i_1 i_2 \ldots i_p} a_{i_1} b_{i_2} \ldots c_{i_p}. \end{align*} $$ (Derivation of following transformation rules)

  • A $p$-tensor in $R^n$ is a multi-linear map $$ \begin{align*} T: \ub{\R^n \times \ldots \times\R^n}_{p-1} \to \R^n \end{align*} $$ such that in a given basis $\set{\v e_i}$, $$ \begin{align*} T(\v a, \v b, \ldots, \v c)_i = T_{i j_1 j_2 \ldots j_p} a_{j_1} b_{j_2} \ldots c_{j_p}. \end{align*} $$

Tensor operations open_in_new

  • If $S, T$ are tensors of the same rank, then so is $S+T$ and $\lambda T$ with $\lambda\in\R$.

  • If $S$ is a $p$-tensor and $T$ is a $q$-tensor, then the tensor product $S\otimes T$ is a $(p+q)$-tensor $$ \begin{align*} (S\otimes T)_{i_1 \ldots i_p j_1 \ldots j_q} = S_{i_1 \ldots i_p} T_{j_1 \ldots j_q}. \end{align*} $$

  • If $T$ is a $p$-tensor, we can construct a $(p-2)$-tensor by contraction $$ \begin{align*} S_{k_1 \ldots k_{p-2}} = \delta_{ij} T_{ijk_1\ldots k_{p-2}}. \end{align*} $$ For rank 2 tensor, the contraction is the trace $A_{ii}$. The dot product is the contraction of the tensor product $\delta_{ij} a_i b_j$.

Proposition (quotient rule for tensors) open_in_new

Let $v_{i_1 \ldots i_p} = T_{i_1 \ldots i_p j_1 \ldots j_q} u_{j_1 \ldots j_q}$.

Then $T$ is a ($p+q$)-tensor iff for every $q$-tensor $u$, $v$ is a $p$-tensor. Proof

Symmetric and anti-symmetric tensor open_in_new

A tensor $T$ is

  • symmetric when $T_{ijp\ldots q} = T_{jip\ldots q}$ or for any other indices;

  • anti-symmetric when $T_{ijp\ldots q} = -T_{jip\ldots q}$ or for any other indices.

(Anti-)symmetry is a basis independent property. (Proof)

Decomposition of a square matrix open_in_new

Any $n\times n$ matrix $B$ can be written as a sum of symmetric and anti-symmetric parts, $$\begin{align*} B_{ij} &= \frac12 (B_{ij} + B_{ji}) + \frac12 (B_{ij} - B_{ji}) \\ B &= \underbrace{\frac12 (B + B^T)}_{\text{symmetric } S} + \underbrace{\frac12 (B - B^T)}_{\text{anti-symmetric } A} \end{align*}$$

A symmetric matrix $S$ can be decomposed an isotropic part and a traceless part, $$\begin{align*} S_{ij} &= \frac1n \mrm{trace}(S)\, \delta_{ij} + \big(S_{ij} - \frac1n \mrm{trace}(S)\, \delta_{ij} \big) \\ S &= \underbrace{\frac1n \mrm{trace}(S)\, I}_\text{isotropic} + \underbrace{\big(S - \frac1n\mrm{trace}(S)\, I\big)}_{\text{traceless, since } \mrm{trace}(\frac1n I) = 1} \end{align*}$$

In $\R^3$, the anti-symmetric part $A$ can be written in terms of a single vector $\vec w$ as $$ A_{ijk} = \epsilon_{ijk}\omega_k = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & \omega_3 & -\omega_2 \\ -\omega_3 & 0 & \omega_1 \\ \omega_2 & -\omega_1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$ which is a general anti-symmetric matrix.

Invariant tensors open_in_new

A tensor $T$ is invariant or isotropic when $T'_{i_1 \ldots i_p} = T_{i_1 \ldots i_p}$ for all $R$.

  • Any rank 0 tensor is isotropic.

  • There is no isotropic rank 1 tensors.

  • $\delta_{ij}$ is an isotropic rank 2 tensor. (Proof)

  • $\epsilon_{ijk}$, the totally anti-symmetric rank 3 tensor, is isotropic under $\mathrm{SO}(3)$. (Proof)

Lemma (only isotropic tensor in R3 of rank under 3) open_in_new

The only non-zero isotropic tensors in $\R^3$ of rank 1,2,3 are $\alpha \delta_{ij}$ and $\beta \epsilon_{ijk}$.

Proof

In fact, all isotropic tensors in $\R^n$ are built from the totally anti-symmetric $n$-tensor $\epsilon_{ijk}$ and the symmetric 2-tensor $\delta_{ij}$, e.g. a general isotropic rank 4 tensor in $\R^3$ is $$ \begin{align*} T_{ijkl} = \alpha\delta_{ij}\delta_{kl} + \beta\delta_{ik}\delta_{jl} + \gamma\delta_{il}\delta_{jk}. \end{align*} $$

Invariant integrals open_in_new

Noticing invariant tensors can simplify integrals. For example for a rotationally invariant volume $V$, $$ \begin{align*} T_{ij\ldots k} = \int_{V} f(r)x_i x_j \ldots x_k \, dV, \quad r = |\v x| \end{align*} $$ is invariant since $$ \begin{align*} T_{ij\ldots k}' &= R_{ip} R_{jq} \ldots R_{kr} T_{pq\ldots r} \\ &= \int_{V} f(r) \, (R_{ip}x_p)(R_{jq}x_q)\ldots(R_{kr}x_r)\, dV \\ &= \int_{V} f(r) \,x_i' x_j' \ldots x_k'\, dV \end{align*} $$ and $r$ and $dV$ are invariant as $V$ is rotationally invariant. Hence $T_{ij\ldots k}$ is proportional to an invariant tensor.

Tensor field open_in_new

A tensor field over $\R^n$ assigns a tensor $T_{ij\ldots k}(\v x)$ to each point $\v x\in\R^3$.

For a $p$-tensor field $T$, we can differentiate it to get a $(p+q)$-tensor field $$ \begin{align*} X_{i_1 \ldots i_q j_1 \ldots j_p}(\v x) = \pdd{x_{i_1}} \ldots \pdd{x_{i_q}} T_{j_1 \ldots j_p}(\v x). \end{align*} $$ (Derivation)

  • This implies $\nabla\phi$ is a vector field.

  • For a vector field $\v F(\v x)$, the tensor field $$ \begin{align*} T_{ij}(\v x) = \pdfd{F_i(\v x)}{x^j} \end{align*} $$ can be decomposed into $$ \begin{align*} T_{ij} = \ub{-\frac{1}{2}\epsilon_{ijk}\left(\nabla\times\v F\right)_k}_\text{anti-symmetric} + \ub{\frac{1}{3}(\nabla\cdot\v F)\delta_{ij}}_{\text{isotropic}} + \ub{\frac{1}{2}\left(\pdfd{F_i}{x^j} + \pdfd{F_j}{x^i}\right) - \frac{1}{3}\left(\nabla\cdot\v F\right)\delta_{ij}}_{\text{symmetric traceless}}. \end{align*} $$ (Derivation)

Physical examples of tensors open_in_new

Conductivity and resisitivity open_in_new

In a material, applying an electric field $\v E$ gives rise to a current $\v J$: $$ \begin{align*} J_i = \sigma_{ij} E_j \iff E_i = \rho_{ij}J_j \end{align*} $$ where $\sigma_{ij}$ is the conductivity tensor and $\rho_{ij} = (\sigma\inv)_{ij}$ is the resistivity tensor.

Isotropic material has invariant $\sigma_{ij}$:

  • in 2D, $\sigma_{ij} = \sigma\delta_{ij}$ where $\sigma$ is the conductivity;

  • in 3D, $\sigma_{ij} = \sigma_{xx} \delta_{ij} + \sigma_{xy} \epsilon_{ij}$ where $\sigma_{xy}$ is the Hall conductivity.

Inertia tensor open_in_new

A rigid body has angular momentum $$ \begin{align*} \v L = I\v \omega \end{align*} $$ where $I$ is the inertia tensor $$ \begin{align*} I_{ij} &= \sum_a m_a \left(|\v x_a|^2 \delta_{ij} - (\v x_a)_i (\v x_a)_j\right) &&\text{for discrete object}, \\ I_{ij} &= \int_{V} \rho(x) \left(|\v x|^2\delta_{ij} - x_i x_j\right)\, dV &&\text{for continuous object}. \end{align*} $$ (Derivation)

$I$ is symmetric, so it is diagonable: $I' = RIR^T = \op{diag}(I_1, I_2, I_3)$ where $R\in\mathrm{SO}(3)$. Every object has a preferred set of axis i.e. the eigenvectors.

Elasticity tensor open_in_new

The strain tensor $e_{ij}$ is a symmetric tensor that describes the deformation of the point $x^i$ in the direction $\vhat x^j$.

The stress tensor $\sigma_{ij}$ is a symmetric tensor that describes the force $F_i$ on the plane perpendicular to $\vhat x_j$

They are related by $$ \begin{align*} \sigma_{ij} = C_{ijkl} e_{kl} \end{align*} $$ where $C_{ijkl}$ is the elasticity tensor.
