Cylindrical polar coordinates are given by $$\begin{align*} x &= \rho \cos \phi & & & \rho&\in[0,\infty) \\ y &= \rho \sin \phi & &\text{with} & \phi&\in[0, 2\pi) \\ z &= z &&& z&\in(-\infty, \infty). \end{align*}$$ The inverse map is $$ \begin{align*} \rho &= \sqrt{x^2+y^2}, \\ \tan\phi &= \frac yx. \end{align*} $$

The orthonormal vectors are $$ \begin{align*} \v e_\rho \equiv \vhat\rho &= (\cos\phi,\sin\phi,0), & h_\rho &= 1, \\ \v e_\phi \equiv \vhat\phi &= (-\sin\phi, \cos\phi,0), & h_\phi &= \rho, \\ \v e_z \equiv\vhat z &= (0,0,1), &h_z &= 1. \end{align*} $$

So the Jacobian is $$ \pdfd{(x,y,z)}{(\rho,\phi,z)} = h_\rho h_\phi h_z = \rho. $$ The derivative stuff are $$\begin{align*} \nabla &= \vhat\rho \pdd \rho + \frac1\rho\vhat\phi\pdd\phi + \vhat z\pdd z, \\ \nabla f &= \vhat\rho \pdfd f \rho + \frac1\rho\vhat\phi\pdfd f\phi + \vhat z\pdfd fz, \\ \nabla\cdot\v F &= \frac1\rho\pdfd{(\rho F_\rho)}\rho + \frac1\rho\pdfd{F_\phi}\phi + \pdfd{F_z}z, \\ \nabla^2 f &= \frac1\rho\pdd\rho\bigg(\rho\pdfd f\rho\bigg) + \frac1{\rho^2}\pdfd[2]f\phi + \pdfd[2]fz. \end{align*}$$
