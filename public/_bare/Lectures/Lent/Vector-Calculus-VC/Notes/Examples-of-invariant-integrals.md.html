Let $V$ be a 3D ball of radius $R$.

- $$ \begin{align*} T_i = \int_{V} \rho(r)\, x_i\, dV \end{align*} $$ This is a rank 1 isotropic tensor, so $T_i = 0$.

(This makes sense physically, since there is no reason that $T_i$ would point in any particular direction.)

- $$ \begin{align*} T_{ij} = \int_{V} \rho(r)\, x_i x_j\, dV \end{align*} $$ This is a rank 2 isotropic tensor, so $T_{ij} = \alpha\delta_{ij}$.

To determine $\alpha$, take the trace: $$ \begin{align*} 3\alpha = T_{ii} = \int_{V} \rho(r) r^2\, dV = 4\pi \int_{0}^{R} r^4 \rho(r)\, dr. \end{align*} $$

  • Let $S = \partial V$. $$ \begin{align*} T_k = a_j \int_{S} dS_i \frac{x_ix_jx_k}{r^5} \end{align*} $$ where $a_j$ is a given vector.

    The integral $\tilde T_{jk} = \int_{S} dS_i x_ix_jx_k/r^5$ is a rank 2 isotropic tensor, so $T_k = \beta a_j \delta_{jk} = \beta a_k$.

    (This makes sense physically, since the only special direction is $\v a$.)

    To determine $\beta$, take trace of $\tilde T_{jk}$: $$ \begin{align*} 3\beta &= \int_{S} dS_i \frac{x_i r^2}{r^5} \\ &= \int_{S} dS_i \frac{x_i}{r^3} \\ &= \frac{1}{R^2} \int_{S} \v n\cdot d\v S \\ &= 4\pi \end{align*} $$ so $\beta = 4\pi/3$.
