Definition (fixed or equilibrium point of an ODE) open_in_new

A fixed point of an ODE $$ \frac{dy}{dt} = f(t,y) $$ is a constant solution $y=c$, i.e. $dy/dt = 0$ for all $t$.

Definition (stability of fixed point) open_in_new

  • A fixed point $y=c$ is stable if whenever $y$ deviates slightly from $c$, $y$ converges to $c$ as $t\to\infty$.

  • It is unstable if whenever $y$ deviates slightly from $c$, $y$ diverges from $c$ as $t\to\infty$.

Using perturbation to find stability of ODE open_in_new

To find out the stability of a fixed point, we consider a small perturbation of $y$ from $c$, $$ y(t) = c + \epsilon(t). $$ So $$ \frac{d\epsilon}{dt} = f(t, c+\epsilon(t)) = \underbrace{f(t, c)}_0 + \epsilon\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\Big|_{t,c} + O(\epsilon^2). $$ Linearising the small $\epsilon$, $$ \frac{d\epsilon}{dt} \approx \epsilon\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\Big|_{t,c}. $$ Then solve for $\epsilon$ to find out behaviour as $t\to\infty$.

Stability in autonomous systems and phase portraits open_in_new

An autonomous system has a differential equation of the form $$ \frac{dy}{dt} = f(y) $$ which does not depend on $t$.

Suppose we have a fixed point $y=c$, i.e. $f(c) = 0$. Then $$ \frac{d\epsilon}{dt} = \epsilon f'(c) + O(\epsilon^2) $$

Lectures notes say that by linearising, we have the solution $$ \epsilon(t) = \epsilon_0 e^{f'(c)t}. $$ In practice, just considering the sign of $f'(c)$, or the first non-zero derivative of $f$, is enough to determine the stability.

Hence the fixed point is

  • stable when $f'(c) < 0$;

  • unstable when $f'(c) > 0$.

A phase diagram shows the direction solutions flow as $t$ increases.

Fixed points in discrete equations open_in_new

Consider a first-order discrete equation which is independent of $n$ $$ x_{n+1} = f(x_n). $$

Definition (fixed point of a discrete equation) open_in_new

A fixed point of a first-order discrete equation is a value $x_n$ such that $$ x_{n+1} = x_n $$ i.e. $$ f(x_n) = x_n. $$

Using perturbation analysis, let $x_f$ be a fixed point and we let $$ x_n = x_f + \epsilon_n. $$ Then $$ x_f + \epsilon_{n+1} = f(x_f + \epsilon_n) = \underbrace{f(x_f)}_{x_f\text{ by fixed point}} + \epsilon_n f'(x_f) + O(\epsilon_n)^2. $$ Neglecting higher order terms, $$ \epsilon_{n+1} \approx \epsilon_n f'(x_f). $$

Therefore, a stable point is

  • stable when $|f'(x_f)| < 1$;

  • unstable when $|f'(x_f)| > 1$.
