Since $H$ is a real symmetric matrix, the eigenvalues are real. Also we can diagonalise with an orthogonal transformation and write $$ \delta\v x H\delta \v x^T = \pmat{\delta x_1 & \delta x_2 & \ldots & \delta x_n} \pmat{\lambda_1 & 0 & \ldots & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda_2 & \ldots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \ldots & \lambda_n} \pmat{\delta x_1 \\ \delta x_2 \\ \vdots \\ \delta x_n} $$ in some orthonormal basis $\set{\v e_1, \v e_2, \ldots, \v e_n}$.

Then we can see that $$\begin{align*} \text{$H$ is positive definite} &\iff \delta\v x H \delta\v x^T > 0 && \forall\delta\v x\neq 0 \\ &\iff \v e_i H\v e_i^T > 0 && \forall i=1,2,\ldots,n \\ &\iff \lambda_i > 0 && \forall i=1,2,\ldots,n. \end{align*}$$ Same for negative definite.

If all eigenvalues of $H$ is non-zero but with mixed signs, then $f$ can increase or decrease depending on the direction, so it is a saddle point.
