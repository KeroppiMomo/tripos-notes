Definition (big O) open_in_new

$f(x)=O(g(x))$ as $x\to x_0$ if
there exists $M>0$ and
there exists $\delta > 0$ such that
for all $x$ where $0<|x-x_0|<\delta$, $$ |f(x)|\leq M|g(x)|. $$

Extending this, $f(x)=O(g(x))$ as $x\to\infty$ if
there exists $M>0$ and

there exists $\delta > 0$, such that

Similar definition for $x\to\infty$.

Definition (Taylor polynomial) open_in_new

For a function $f(x)$ that is $n$-times differentiable at $x_0$, its $n$-th order Taylor polynomial about $x_0$ is $$ \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{f^{(k)}(x_0)}{k!}(x-x_0)^k. $$

Theorem (Taylor theorem) open_in_new

For a function $f(x)$ that is $n$ times differentiable at $x_0$, it differs from its $n$-th order Taylor polynomial by $o((x-x_0)^n)$ as $x\to x_0$: $$ f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{f^{(k)}(x_0)(x-x_0)^k}{k!} + E_n $$ with $E_n = o((x-x_0)^n)$.

Theorem (stronger Taylor theorem) open_in_new

If $f^{(n+1)}$ exists and is continuous for all $(x_0,x)$, then $$ E_n = O((x-x_0)^{n+1}).$$

Proof

Definition (Taylor series) open_in_new

For a function $f(x)$ which is infinitely differentiable at $x_0$, then the Taylor series about $x_0$ is $$ \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(k)}(x_0)}{k!}(x-x_0)^k. $$

L'Hôpital's rule open_in_new

Considering the limit of the ratio of two functions $\lim_{x\to x_0} f(x)/g(x)$, if

  • $f(x_0)=g(x_0)=0$ or $\pm\infty$;

  • $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ are differentiable at an open interval $I$ around $x_0$ possibly except $x_0$;

  • $g'(x)\neq 0$ for all $x\in I$ but $x\neq x_0$; and

  • $\lim_{x\to x_0} f'(x)/g'(x)$ exists,

then $$ \lim_{x\to x_0} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x\to x_0}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}. $$

Definition (partial differentiation) open_in_new

Given function $f(x,y)$, the partial derivative of $f$ with respect to $x$ is $$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\Big|_y = \lim_{\delta x\to 0} \frac{f(x+\delta x, y) - f(x,y)}{\delta x} $$ and that with respect to $y$ is $$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\Big|_x = \lim_{\delta y\to 0} \frac{f(x, y+\delta y) - f(x,y)}{\delta y}. $$

Notation (partial differentiation) open_in_new

We usually omit the evaluation line and write $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\Big|_y \equiv \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \equiv f_x.$$

Mixed partial differentiation is written as $$\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\Big(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\Big) \equiv \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x\partial y} \equiv f_{yx}.$$

Theorem (Schwarz theorem) open_in_new

If $\partial^2 f/\partial x\partial y$ is continuous, then $$\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x\partial y} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y\partial x}.$$

Exact equation open_in_new

An ODE $P(x,y) \,dx + Q(x,y)\,dy = 0$ is exact if there exists $f(x,y)$ such that $$ df = P(x,y)\,dx + Q(x,y)\,dy. $$ Then $df=0$ so $f(x,y) =$ constant is the solution.

Therefore we need to find $f$ such that $$\begin{cases} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = P(x,y), \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = Q(x,y). \end{cases}$$

Condition for exact equation open_in_new

Check this condition in your head:

Note that $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x\partial y} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y\partial x}$, so a necessary condition for an exact equation is $$ \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}. $$

The theorem below says this is a sufficient condition under certain domains.

Theorem (sufficient condition for exact differential in simply-connected domain) open_in_new

If $$\begin{align*} \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} \end{align*}$$ throughout a simply connected domain $\mathcal D$, then $P\,dx + Q\,dy$ is an exact differential of single-valued $f(x,y)$ in $\mathcal D$.

Fixed points and stability open_in_new

Definition (fixed or equilibrium point of an ODE) open_in_new

A fixed point of an ODE $$ \frac{dy}{dt} = f(t,y) $$ is a constant solution $y=c$, i.e. $dy/dt = 0$ for all $t$.

Definition (stability of fixed point) open_in_new

  • A fixed point $y=c$ is stable if whenever $y$ deviates slightly from $c$, $y$ converges to $c$ as $t\to\infty$.

  • It is unstable if whenever $y$ deviates slightly from $c$, $y$ diverges from $c$ as $t\to\infty$.

Using perturbation to find stability of ODE open_in_new

To find out the stability of a fixed point, we consider a small perturbation of $y$ from $c$, $$ y(t) = c + \epsilon(t). $$ So $$ \frac{d\epsilon}{dt} = f(t, c+\epsilon(t)) = \underbrace{f(t, c)}_0 + \epsilon\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\Big|_{t,c} + O(\epsilon^2). $$ Linearising the small $\epsilon$, $$ \frac{d\epsilon}{dt} \approx \epsilon\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\Big|_{t,c}. $$ Then solve for $\epsilon$ to find out behaviour as $t\to\infty$.

Stability in autonomous systems and phase portraits open_in_new

An autonomous system has a differential equation of the form $$ \frac{dy}{dt} = f(y) $$ which does not depend on $t$.

Suppose we have a fixed point $y=c$, i.e. $f(c) = 0$. Then $$ \frac{d\epsilon}{dt} = \epsilon f'(c) + O(\epsilon^2) $$

Lectures notes say that by linearising, we have the solution $$ \epsilon(t) = \epsilon_0 e^{f'(c)t}. $$ In practice, just considering the sign of $f'(c)$, or the first non-zero derivative of $f$, is enough to determine the stability.

Hence the fixed point is

  • stable when $f'(c) < 0$;

  • unstable when $f'(c) > 0$.

A phase diagram shows the direction solutions flow as $t$ increases.

Fixed points in discrete equations open_in_new

Consider a first-order discrete equation which is independent of $n$ $$ x_{n+1} = f(x_n). $$

Definition (fixed point of a discrete equation) open_in_new

A fixed point of a first-order discrete equation is a value $x_n$ such that $$ x_{n+1} = x_n $$ i.e. $$ f(x_n) = x_n. $$

Using perturbation analysis, let $x_f$ be a fixed point and we let $$ x_n = x_f + \epsilon_n. $$ Then $$ x_f + \epsilon_{n+1} = f(x_f + \epsilon_n) = \underbrace{f(x_f)}_{x_f\text{ by fixed point}} + \epsilon_n f'(x_f) + O(\epsilon_n)^2. $$ Neglecting higher order terms, $$ \epsilon_{n+1} \approx \epsilon_n f'(x_f). $$

Therefore, a stable point is

  • stable when $|f'(x_f)| < 1$;

  • unstable when $|f'(x_f)| > 1$.

Definition (linear differential operator) open_in_new

An operator $\mathcal D$ is linear if for any functions $y_1(x), y_2(x)$ and constants $\alpha,\beta$, $$ \mathcal D(\alpha y_1 + \beta y_2) = \alpha\mathcal D y_1 + \beta\mathcal D y_2. $$

Definition (linearly independence of functions) open_in_new

A set of $N$ functions $\{f_i(x)\}$ is linearly dependent if $$ \sum_{i=1}^N c_i f_i(x) = 0 $$ for $N$ constants $c_i$ not all zero.

Otherwise the functions are linearly independent.

General form $$ a \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + b\frac{dy}{dx} + cy = f(x). $$

Homogenous solution of second-order ODE with constant coefficient open_in_new

$$ a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + b\frac{dy}{dx} + cy = 0. $$ Solution has the form $y(x) = e^{\lambda x}$, so the characteristic equation is $$ a\lambda^2 + b\lambda + c = 0. $$

  • If the roots $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$, then $$ y(x) = Ae^{\lambda_1 x} + Be^{\lambda_2 x}. $$

  • If the roots $\lambda_1 = \lambda_2 = \lambda$, then $$ y(x) = (A + Bx)e^{\lambda x}. $$

Phase space open_in_new

For an $n$-th order linear ODE $$ y^{(n)} + p(x) y^{(n-1)} + \ldots + q(x) y = f(x), $$ $y^{(n)}(x_0)$ is determined by $y(x_0), y'(x_0), y''(x_0), \ldots, y^{(n-1)}(x_0)$. Similarly by differentiating the ODE, we can determine higher derivatives.

Therefore we can construct a Taylor series of $y(x)$ about $x_0$ if $y(x_0), y'(x_0), y''(x_0), \ldots, y^{(n-1)}(x_0)$ are specified. By some theorem, a solution $y(x)$ is determined by these initial conditions.

The state of system is fully specified at any $x$ by the solution vector $$ \vec Y(x) = \begin{pmatrix} y(x) \\ y'(x) \\ y''(x) \\ \vdots \\ y^{(n-1)}(x) \end{pmatrix}. $$ This means at any $x=x_0$, $\vec Y(x_0)$, a point in the $n$ dimensional phase space, determines the full solution $y(x)$.

Definition (Wronskian) open_in_new

The Wronskian of $n$ functions $y_i(x)$ for $i=1,2,\ldots, n$ is the determinant of the fundamental matrix, defined by $$ W(x) = \begin{vmatrix} y_1 & y_2 & \ldots & y_n \\ y_1' & y_2' & \ldots & y_n' \\ y_1'' & y_2'' & \ldots & y_n'' \\ \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ y_1^{(n-1)} & y_2^{(n-1)} & \ldots & y_n^{(n-1)} \end{vmatrix}. $$

Proposition (linear dependence and zero Wronskian) open_in_new

If $n$ functions $\{y_i(x)\}$ are linearly dependent, then their Wronskian $W(x) = 0$ for all $x$.

Its contraposition is that if $W(x) \neq 0$, then $\{y_i(x)\}$ are linearly independent.

Proof

Theorem (Abel's theorem) open_in_new

Given any two solutions of a linear homogenous second-order ODE $$ y'' + p(x) y' + q(x) y = 0, $$ if $p(x),q(x)$ are continuous on an interval $I$, then Abel's identity states that the Wronskian satisfies Abel's identity $$ W(x) = W(x_0) \exp\Big(-\int_{x_0}^x p(u) \,du\Big). $$

In particular either

  • $W(x) = 0$ for all $x\in I$; or

  • $W(x) \neq 0$ for all $x\in I$.

Proof

This tells us that two solution vectors $\vec Y_1(x), \vec Y_2(x)$ are either always collinear in the phase space or never as $x$ varies. Therefore, if $\vec Y_1(x_0), \vec Y_2(x_0)$ form a basis, then $\vec Y_1(x), \vec Y_2(x)$ also form a basis at any $x$.

Corollary (Wronskian and missing y' term) open_in_new

For a homogenous linear second-order ODE with missing $y'$ term $$ y'' + q(x) y = 0, $$ the Wronskian $W(x)$ is a constant function.

Proof

Reduction of order using Abel's theorem open_in_new

By Abel's identity, $$ y_1 y_2' - y_1' y_2 = W(x_0) \exp\Big(-\int_{x_0}^x p(u)\,du\Big), $$ which, if $y_1$ is known, gives a first-order linear ODE of $y_2$. Using quotient rule, $$ \frac{d}{dx}\frac{y_2}{y_1} = \frac{W(x_0)}{y_1^2}\exp\Big(-\int_{x_0}^x p(u)\,du\Big) $$ $$ y_2(x) = y_1(x) \int^x \frac{\exp\Big(-\int^\xi p(u)\,du\Big)}{y_1(\xi)^2}\,d\xi, $$ which is equivalent to the reduction of order formula.

Forcing oscillating system open_in_new

We investigate in the equation $$ m\ddot y + b\dot y + ky = F(t). $$

We usually write it in terms of the dimensionless time $\tau = \omega_0 t$, where $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$, $$ y'' + 2\kappa y' + y = f(\tau) $$ where $\kappa = b/2m\omega_0$.

For complimentary functions, depending on the value of $\kappa$, the solution has different behaviours:

  • For $\kappa < 1$, light damping/underdamping: $$ y(t) = e^{-\kappa\omega_0 t}(A\sin\omega_{free}t + B\cos\omega_{free}t), $$ where $\omega_{free} = \sqrt{1-\kappa^2}\ \omega_0$.

  • For $\kappa = 1$, critical damping: $$ y(t) = e^{-\kappa\omega_0t}(A + Bt). $$

  • For $\kappa > 1$, heavy damping/overdamping: $$ y(t) = \underbrace{Ae^{-(\kappa - \sqrt{\kappa^2 - 1})\omega_0 t)}}_\text{dominates long-term} + Be^{-(\kappa + \sqrt{\kappa^2 - 1})\omega_0 t}.$$

Derivation of non-forcing oscillating system

For the general solution,

  • in the beginning, the solution is given by both complimentary functions and particular integral, which is called the transient response.

  • But since all complimentary functions eventually decay, the general solution is dominated by the particular integral in the long run. This is the steady-state response.

Definition (Dirac delta function) open_in_new

Consider any family of functions $D(t; \epsilon)$ satisfying

  • $\lim_{\epsilon\to 0} D(t;\epsilon) = 0$ for all $t\neq 0$;

  • $\int_{-\infty}^\infty D(t;\epsilon)\,dt = 1$ for all $\epsilon$.

We define the Dirac delta function as $$ \delta(x) \equiv \lim_{\epsilon\to 0} D(x;\epsilon). $$

We just focus on these properties:

  • $\delta(x) = 0$ for all $x\neq 0$;

  • $\int_{-\infty}^\infty \delta(x)\, dt = 1$;

  • If $g(x)$ is continuous at $x=0$, then $\int_{-\infty}^\infty g(x) \delta(x) \, dt = g(0)$. It follows that $$ \int_a^b g(x) \delta(x-x_0)\,dt = \begin{cases} g(x_0), & a < x < b, \\ 0, & x < a \text{ or } x > b, \\ \text{undefined}, & x=a \text{ or } x=b. \end{cases} $$

We define the Heaviside step function to be $$ H(x) = \int_{-\infty}^x \delta(t)\, dt, $$ so $$\begin{align*} H(x) &= \begin{cases} 0, & x<0, \\ 1, & x>0, \\ \text{undefined}, &x=0; \end{cases} \\ \frac{dH}{dx} &= \delta(x). \end{align*}$$

$$ y'' + p(x) y' + q(x) y = H(x) $$ If we assume $y'' \sim H(x)$, then $y'\sim xH(x)$ and $y\sim x^2 H(x)$, which are continuous. So we have $$ \lim_{\epsilon\to 0} [y']_{-\epsilon}^{\epsilon} = 0 = \lim_{\epsilon\to 0} [y]_{-\epsilon}^\epsilon. $$

Discrete solution of a differential equation open_in_new

Consider discretising the equation at points $\se{x_n}$ spaced by $h$ and take $y_n = y(x_n)$. We can estimate the derivative by the forward Euler scheme $$ \frac{dy}{dx}\Big|_{x_n} \approx \frac{y_{n+1} - y_n}{h}. $$

For second-order derivative, $$\begin{align*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\Big|_{x_n} &\approx \frac{dy/dx\big|_{x_{n+1}} - dy/dx\big|_{x_n}}{h} \\ &= \frac{y_{n+2} - 2y_{n+1} + y_n}{h^2}. \end{align*}$$

Classification of singular points open_in_new

Definition (ordinary and singular point) open_in_new

If $q(x)/p(x)$ and $r(x)/p(x)$ are analytic at $x=x_0$, i.e. have Taylor series around $x=x_0$, then $x=x_0$ is called an ordinary point. Otherwise it is a singular point.

Definition (regular and irregular singular point) open_in_new

Suppose $x=x_0$ is a singular point. If the equation can be written as $$ P(x) (x-x_0)^2 y'' + Q(x) (x-x_0) y' + R(x) y = 0 $$ where $Q(x)/P(x)$ and $R(x)/P(x)$ are analytic at $x=x_0$, then $x=x_0$ is a regular singular point. Otherwise it is an irregular singular point.

Equivalently, $x=x_0$ is a regular singular point iff $$ \frac{(x-x_0) q(x)}{p(x)}\quad\text{and}\quad\frac{(x-x_0)^2 r(x)}{p(x)} $$ are analytic at $x=x_0$.

Method of Frobenius open_in_new

This method applies for solutions about a regular singular point.

The solutions depend on the roots $\sigma_1, \sigma_2$ of the indicial equation:

  • If $\sigma_2 - \sigma_1\not\in \Z$, then we have two linearly independent Frobenius series solution, $$ y_1(x) = (x - x_0)^{\sigma_1} \sumi n0\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n, $$ $$ y_2(x) = (x - x_0)^{\sigma_2} \sumi n0\infty b_n(x-x_0)^n. $$

  • If $\sigma_2 - \sigma_1 \in\Z$, suppose $\sigma_1 \leq \sigma_2$. Then we have one Frobenius series solution $$ y_1(x) = (x-x_0)^{\sigma_2} \sumi n0\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n $$ and another solution involving $\ln$, $$ y_2(x) = (x-x_0)^{\sigma_1} \sumi n0\infty b_n(x-x_0)^n + cy_1(x)\ln(x-x_0). $$ for some constant $c$ determined by $b_0$.

Multivariate functions open_in_new

Directional derivative open_in_new

Definition (gradient) open_in_new

For a function $f(x,y)$, its gradient is $$ \mrm{grad}(f) \equiv \v\nabla f \equiv \Big(\pdfd fx, \pdfd fy\Big).$$

Definition (directional derivative) open_in_new

The directional derivative of $f(x,y)$ in the direction $\vhat s$ is $$ \dfd fs = \vhat s\cdot\v\nabla f. $$

Properties of gradient vector open_in_new

  • Direction of $\v\nabla f$ is that in which $f$ increases most rapidly.

  • Magnitude of $\v\nabla f$ is the maximum rate of change of $f$.

  • When $0 = \dfd fs = \vhat s\cdot\v\nabla f$, we have $\hat s$ being along a contour of $f$, so $\v\nabla f$ is perpendicular to the contours of $f$.

Taylor series for multivariate functions open_in_new

The first three terms of the Taylor series around $\v x = \v x_0$ is $$ f(\v x_0 + \delta\v x) = f(\v x_0) + \delta\v x\cdot\v\nabla f\big|_{\v x_0} + \frac12 \delta\v x\ (\vnabla\vnabla f)\big|_{\v x_0}\ \delta\v x^T + \ldots $$ where $\delta\v x$ is a row vector and $\vnabla\vnabla f$ is the Hessian matrix $$ H \equiv \vnabla\vnabla f \equiv \pmat{f_{xx} & f_{xy} \\ f_{yx} & f_{yy}}. $$

If $f_{xy}$ is continuous, then $f_{xy} = f_{yx}$ so $H$ is symmetric.

What is on the lecture notes has $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ swapped compared to Wikipedia, but I guess no one really cares which is which since it's usually symmetric.

Nature of stationary point open_in_new

Definition (stationary point) open_in_new

$\v x_0$ is a stationary point of a function $f(\v x)$ if $$ \vnabla f\big|_{\v x_0} = \v 0. $$

Equivalently, the directional derivative $\dfd fs\big|_{\v x_0} = 0$ for all $\vhat s$.

Definition (positive definite, negative definite, indefinite) open_in_new

A real symmetric matrix $H$ is

  • positive definite if $\v x H\v x^T > 0$ for all real (row) vector $\v x\neq 0$;

  • negative definite if $\v x H\v x^T < 0$ for all real (row) vector $\v x\neq 0$; or

  • indefinite if otherwise.

Definiteness of Hessian and stationary point open_in_new

The nature of a stationary point can sometimes be determined by the Hessian $H$:

  • $H$ is positive definite $\implies$ local minimum.

  • $H$ is negative definite $\implies$ local maximum.

  • If $H$ is indefinite, then we don't know.

(Derivation)

Determining definiteness using eigenvalues open_in_new

Let $\lambda_i$ be the eigenvalues of $H$.

  • $\lambda_i > 0$ for all $i$ $\iff$ $H$ is positive definite $\implies$ local minimum;

  • $\lambda_i < 0$ for all $i$ $\iff$ $H$ is negative definite $\implies$ local maximum;

  • $\lambda_i\neq 0$ for all $i$ and with mixed signs $\implies$ saddle point;

  • Otherwise don't know.

(Derivation)

Determining definiteness using signature open_in_new

Definition (signature of matrix) open_in_new

The signature of a matrix $H$ is the pattern of signs of the ordered subdeterminant of the leading principle minors of $H$.

The following is a human-readable definition: for an $n\times n$ matrix $H$, its signature is the list of the signs of $$ |H_1|, |H_2|, |H_3|, \ldots, |H_n| $$ where $$\begin{align*} H_1 &= \pmat{ H_{11} }, \\ H_2 &= \pmat{H_{11} & H_{12} \\ H_{21} & H_{22}}, \\ H_3 &= \pmat{H_{11} & H_{12} & H_{13} \\ H_{21} & H_{22} & H_{23} \\ H_{31} & H_{32} & H_{33} \\ }, \\ \vdots \\ H_n &= H = \pmat{H_{11} & H_{12} & \ldots & H_{1n} \\ H_{21} & H_{22} & \ldots & H_{2n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ H_{n1} & H_{n2} & \ldots & H_{nn}}. \end{align*}$$

Proposition (Sylvester's criterion) open_in_new

For a real symmetric $n\times n$ matrix $H$,

  • $H$ is positive definite $\iff$ signature is $+, +, +, \ldots, +$.

  • $H$ is negative definite $\iff$ signature is $-, +, -, +, \ldots, (-1)^n$.

Contours near stationary points open_in_new

If the eigenvalues of $H$ is non-zero,

  • near a local maximum or minimum, the contours are elliptical;

  • near a saddle point, the contours are hyperbolic.

Systems of first-order ODEs open_in_new

Linear system of ODEs open_in_new

Consider two functions $y_1$ and $y_2$, $$ \begin{cases} \dot y_1 &= ay_1 + by_2 + f_1(t), \\ \dot y_2 &= cy_1 + dy_2 + f_2(t). \end{cases} $$

Solving systems of linear ODEs by substitution open_in_new

$$ \begin{align*} \ddot y_1 &= a\dot y_1 + b(cy_1 + dy_2 + f_2) + \dot f_1 \\ &= a\dot y_1 + bcy_1 + d(by_2) + bf_2 + \dot f_1 \\ &= a\dot y_1 + bcy_1 + d(\dot y_1 - ay_1 - f_1) + bf_2 + \dot f_1 \\ \end{align*} $$ and this is a linear second-order ODE with constant coefficient.

Solving systems of linear ODEs by matrix methods open_in_new

We can write this in vector form $$ \dv Y = M\v Y + \v F$$ where $$ \v Y = \pmat{y_1\\y_2}, \v F = \pmat{ f_1 \\ f_2 }, M = \pmat{a&b \\ c&d}. $$

To look for complementary solution $\v Y_c$, we guess $$ \v Y_c = \v v e^{\lambda t}.$$ Then $$ M\v v = \lambda\v v, $$ so $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $M$ and $\v v$ is the eigenvector.

If there are $n$ distinct eigenvalues, then there will be $n$ linearly independent complimentary functions.

If the forcing term is a complementary function, then try $\v Y_p = (\v a + \v bt)e^{\lambda t}$.

Non-linear autonomous systems open_in_new

Consider an autonomous system for 2 non-linear, first-order ODEs $$\begin{cases} \dot x &= f(x,y), \\ \dot y &= g(x,y). \end{cases}$$

Definition (fixed or equilibrium point of system of autonomous ODEs) open_in_new

A fixed point of the system $$\begin{cases} \dot x &= f(x,y), \\ \dot y &= g(x,y) \end{cases}$$ is a point at which $\dot x = \dot y = 0$. This means $$ f(x,y) = g(x,y) = 0. $$

Using perturbation to find stability of system of autonomous ODEs open_in_new

Let $$\begin{align*} x(t) &= x_0 + \xi(t), &&\xi(t) \ll 1, \\ y(t) &= y_0 + \eta(t), &&\eta(t) \ll 1, \end{align*}$$ where $(x_0, y_0)$ is a fixed point. Then by Taylor expanding, we obtain $$ \pmat{\dot\xi \\ \dot\eta} = \ub{\pmat{\partial f/\partial x & \partial f/\partial y \\ \partial g/\partial x & \partial g/\partial y}}_M\Bigg|_{x_0, y_0} \pmat{\xi \\ \eta},$$ which is a linear system of homogenous ODEs, so the eigenvalue of $M$ determines the stability of the fixed point. First-order wave equation open_in_new

Consider the equation $$ \pdfd\psi t - c\pdfd\psi x = f(t). $$ By setting $x = x_0 - ct$ (characteristic), and considering $\psi(x(t), t)$, the equation becomes $$ \dfd\psi t = f(t). $$ (Derivation)

Integrating gives $$ \psi(x, t) = \int f(t)\, dt + g(x_0) = \int f(t)\,dt + g(x + ct) $$ where $g$ is the integration constant, which can be different for each characteristic.

Second-order wave equation open_in_new

Consider $$ \pdfd[2]\psi t - c^2\pdfd[2]\psi x = 0. $$

This has general solution $$ \psi(x,t) = f(x+ct) + g(x-ct). $$ (Derivation)
