The additional assumption is that $f'(x)$ and $g'(x)$ are continuous at $x_0$.

We expand $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ as follows: $$\begin{align*} f(x) &= \underbrace{f(x_0)}_0 + (x-x_0) f'(x_0) + o(x-x_0) \\ g(x) &= \underbrace{g(x_0)}_0 + (x-x_0) g'(x_0) + o(x-x_0) \end{align*}$$ so $$\begin{align*} \lim_{x\to x_0} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} &= \lim_{x\to x_0}\frac{(x-x_0) f'(x_0) + o(x-x_0)}{(x-x_0)g'(x_0) + o(x-x_0)} \\ &= \lim_{x\to x_0} \frac{f'(x_0) + \frac{o(x-x_0)}{x-x_0}}{g'(x_0) + \frac{o(x-x_0)}{x-x_0}}. \end{align*}$$

Note that the limit of the denominator $$ \lim_{x\to x_0} \Big(g(x_0) + \frac{o(x-x_0)}{x-x_0}\Big) = g(x_0) \neq 0, $$ so $$\begin{align*} \lim_{x\to x_0} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} &= \frac{\lim_{x\to x_0} \big(f'(x_0) + \frac{o(x-x_0)}{x-x_0}\big)}{\lim_{x\to x_0}\big(g'(x_0) + \frac{o(x-x_0)}{x-x_0}\big)} \\ &= \frac{f'(x_0)}{g'(x_0)} \\ &= \frac{\lim_{x\to x_0} f'(x)}{\lim_{x\to x_0} g'(x)} \\ &= \lim_{x\to x_0} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}. \end{align*}$$
