Definition (differentiation) open_in_new

Define the derivative of a function $f(x)$ with respect to its argument $x$ as the function $$ \frac{df}{dx} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}. $$

Notation (differentiation) open_in_new

$$ \underbrace{\frac{df}{dx}}_\text{Leibniz} = \underbrace{f'(x)}_\text{Lagrange} = \underbrace{\dot f(x)}_\text{Newton (for time)}$$

$$\frac{d^2f}{dx^2} = f''(x) = \ddot f(x)$$

$$ \frac{d^nf}{dx^n} = f^{(n)}(x) $$

Definition (big O) open_in_new

$f(x)=O(g(x))$ as $x\to x_0$ if
there exists $M>0$ and
there exists $\delta > 0$ such that
for all $x$ where $0<|x-x_0|<\delta$, $$ |f(x)|\leq M|g(x)|. $$

Extending this, $f(x)=O(g(x))$ as $x\to\infty$ if
there exists $M>0$ and
there exists $X>0$ such that
for all $x > X$, $$ |f(x)|\leq M|g(x)|. $$

Definition (little o) open_in_new

$f(x) = o(g(x))$ as $x\to x_0$ if
for all $\epsilon>0$,
there exists $\delta > 0$, such that
for all $x$ where $0<|x-x_0|<\delta$, $$ |f(x)|\leq \epsilon|g(x)|. $$

Similar definition for $x\to\infty$.

Proposition (limit of ratio and little o) open_in_new

For $g(x)\neq 0$, $f(x) = o(g(x))$ as $x\to x_0$ iff $$ \lim_{x\to x_0} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = 0. $$

Definition (Taylor polynomial) open_in_new

For a function $f(x)$ that is $n$-times differentiable at $x_0$, its $n$-th order Taylor polynomial about $x_0$ is $$ \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{f^{(k)}(x_0)}{k!}(x-x_0)^k. $$

Theorem (Taylor theorem) open_in_new

For a function $f(x)$ that is $n$ times differentiable at $x_0$, it differs from its $n$-th order Taylor polynomial by $o((x-x_0)^n)$ as $x\to x_0$: $$ f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{f^{(k)}(x_0)(x-x_0)^k}{k!} + E_n $$ with $E_n = o((x-x_0)^n)$.

Theorem (stronger Taylor theorem) open_in_new

If $f^{(n+1)}$ exists and is continuous for all $(x_0,x)$, then $$ E_n = O((x-x_0)^{n+1}).$$

Proof

Definition (Taylor series) open_in_new

For a function $f(x)$ which is infinitely differentiable at $x_0$, then the Taylor series about $x_0$ is $$ \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(k)}(x_0)}{k!}(x-x_0)^k. $$

L'Hôpital's rule open_in_new

Considering the limit of the ratio of two functions $\lim_{x\to x_0} f(x)/g(x)$, if

  • $f(x_0)=g(x_0)=0$ or $\pm\infty$;

  • $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ are differentiable at an open interval $I$ around $x_0$ possibly except $x_0$;

  • $g'(x)\neq 0$ for all $x\in I$ but $x\neq x_0$; and

  • $\lim_{x\to x_0} f'(x)/g'(x)$ exists,

then $$ \lim_{x\to x_0} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x\to x_0}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}. $$

Definition (partial differentiation) open_in_new

Given function $f(x,y)$, the partial derivative of $f$ with respect to $x$ is $$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\Big|_y = \lim_{\delta x\to 0} \frac{f(x+\delta x, y) - f(x,y)}{\delta x} $$ and that with respect to $y$ is $$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\Big|_x = \lim_{\delta y\to 0} \frac{f(x, y+\delta y) - f(x,y)}{\delta y}. $$

Notation (partial differentiation) open_in_new

We usually omit the evaluation line and write $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\Big|_y \equiv \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \equiv f_x.$$

Mixed partial differentiation is written as $$\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\Big(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\Big) \equiv \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x\partial y} \equiv f_{yx}.$$

Theorem (Schwarz theorem) open_in_new

If $\partial^2 f/\partial x\partial y$ is continuous, then $$\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x\partial y} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y\partial x}.$$

Theorem (multi-variate chain rule) open_in_new

For a function $f(x,y)$ where $x$ and $y$ are functions of $t$, $$ \frac{df}{dt} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt}. $$

Proof

Integrals of a multi-variate function open_in_new

For a function $f(x,y)$, $$ \Delta f = \int df = \int\Big(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}dx + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}dy\Big) $$

This does not depend on the path, only on the start and end points.

Implicit differentiation open_in_new

Define $$ \frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\Big|_z $$ be the partial derivative of $y$ with respect to $x$, holding $z$ fixed. This means that $$ \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\Big|_z = 0. $$

Proposition (partial derivative of implicit equation) open_in_new

For a general implicit equation $f(x,y,z) = c$, $$\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\Big|_z = -\frac{\partial f/\partial x\big|_{y,z}}{\partial f/\partial y\big|_{x,z}}.$$

Proof
