Suppose $G\curvearrowright X$. For any $g\in G$, consider $$\begin{align*} t_g : X&\to X \\ x&\mapsto gx. \end{align*}$$

To show $t_g$ is a bijection, notice that for all $x\in X$, $$\begin{align*} t_{g^{-1}}\big(t_g(x)\big) &= t_{g^{-1}}(gx) \\ &= g^{-1}(gx) \\ &= (g^{-1}g)x \\ &= ex \\ &= x \end{align*}$$ so $t_{g^{-1}} = (t_g)^{-1}$. Therefore $t_g$ is a bijection and so $t_g \in\mathrm{Sym}(X)$.

Define a function $$\begin{align*} \phi : G &\to \mathrm{Sym}(X) \\ g &\mapsto t_g. \end{align*}$$ To show that $\phi$ is a homomorphism, note that for all $g,h\in G, x\in X$, $$\begin{align*} (t_g\circ t_h)(x) &= t_g(hx) \\ &= g(hx) \\ &= (gh)x \\ &= t_{gh}(x), \end{align*}$$ so $t_g \circ t_h = t_{gh}$ and hence $\phi(g)\phi(h) = \phi(gh)$. Therefore $\phi$ is a homomorphism.

Conversely, given a homomorphism $$ \phi: G \to \mathrm{Sym}(X), $$ define an action $G\curvearrowright X$ as $$ gx \mapsto \phi(g)(x). $$ This is an action because

  • for all $x\in X$, $ex = \phi(e)(x) = \mathrm{Id}_X(x) = x$;

  • for all $g,h\in G$ and $x\in X$, $$\begin{align*} (gh)x &= \phi(gh)(x) \\ &= (\phi(g)\circ\phi(h))(x) \\ &= \phi(g)\big(\phi(h)(x)\big) \\ &= \phi(g)(hx) \\ &= g(hx). \end{align*}$$
