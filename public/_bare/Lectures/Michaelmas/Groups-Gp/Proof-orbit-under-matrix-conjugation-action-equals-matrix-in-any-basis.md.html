A basis $\set{v_1, \ldots, v_n}$ for $V$ defines an isomorphism of vector spaces $$\begin{align*} \phi :&& \R^n &\to V \\ &&(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n) &\mapsto \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i v_i. \end{align*}$$

The hypothesis that $A$ represents $\alpha$ in this basis means that $$ A = \phi\inv \circ \alpha\circ \phi $$ so $$ \alpha = \phi\circ A\circ \phi\inv. $$

Likewise another basis $\set{u_1, \ldots, u_n}$ corresponds to an isomorphism $$ \psi: \R^n \to V $$ and $B\in M_n(\R)$ represents $\alpha$ in this basis if $$\begin{align*} B &= \psi\inv\circ\alpha\circ\psi \\ &= \psi\inv\circ(\phi\circ A\circ \phi\inv)\circ \psi \\ &= (\psi\inv\circ \phi)\circ A\circ(\psi\inv\circ\phi)\inv \\ &= PAP\inv \end{align*}$$ where $P = \psi\inv\circ\phi$. $P\in\gl n\R$ since $\psi\inv\circ\psi$ is an isomorphism so it is invertible. Hence every matrix representing $\alpha$ is in $\gl n\R A$.

Conversely, suppose $B = PAP\inv$ where $P\in\gl n\R A$. Then setting $\psi = \phi\circ P\inv$ defines a basis $$ \sb{u_i = \psi(e_i)}{\text{standard basis vector } e_i \text{ of } \R^n} $$ for $V$. So $B$ is the matrix for $\alpha$ in this basis.
