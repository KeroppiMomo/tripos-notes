Definition (permutation) open_in_new

A permutation is a bijective function $f: X\to X$. This means that $f$ has an inverse $f^{-1}:X\to X$ where $$ f\circ f^{-1} = Id_X = f^{-1}\circ f. $$

Definition (symmetric groups) open_in_new

$\mathrm{Sym}(X)$ is defined to be the set of all permutations of $X$.

Notation (permutation) open_in_new

For $\rho\in S_n$, we use the notation $$ \rho = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 & \ldots & n \\ \rho(1) & \rho(2) & \rho(3) & \ldots & \rho(n) \end{pmatrix}. $$

We often omit fixed points, i.e. $k$ such that $\rho(k) = k$.

Definition (q-cycle) open_in_new

A permutation $\mu\in S_n$ is a $q$-cycle if for some distinct $a_1,\ldots,a_q = 1,2,\ldots, n$, $$ \mu = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & \ldots & a_{q-1} & a_q \\ a_2 & a_3 & \ldots & a_q & a_1 \end{pmatrix}.$$ We denote this as $$ \mu = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & \ldots & a_q \end{pmatrix}. $$ A 2-cycle is called a transposition.

Definition (disjoint cycles) open_in_new

Two cycles $\pmat{a_1 & \ldots & a_k}$ and $\pmat{b_1 & \ldots & b_l}$ are called disjoint if $a_i \neq b_j$ for all $i,j$.

Definition (disjoint permutation) open_in_new

Two permutations $\mu,\nu\in S_n$ are disjoint when for all $x=1,2,\ldots,n$, if $\mu(x) \neq x$, then $\nu(x) = x$, and similarly if $\nu(x) \neq x$, then $\mu(x) = x$.

Proposition (disjoint permutations commute) open_in_new

For two permutations $\mu,\nu\in S_n$, if they are disjoint, then $$ \mu\circ\nu = \nu\circ\mu. $$

Proof

Theorem (disjoint cycles) open_in_new

Every $\sigma\in S_n$ can be written as a product of disjoint cycles.

The expression is unique up to

  • switching the elements of each cycle; and

  • permuting the cycles.

Proof

Definition (cycle type) open_in_new

If $\sigma = \pmat{a_1^1 & a_2^1 & \ldots & a_{k_1}^1} \ldots \pmat{a_1^l & a_2^l & \ldots & a_{k_l}^l}$ are disjoint cycles, then $\sigma$ is called a $(k_1, k_2, \ldots, k_l)$-cycle. The set of numbers $\set{k_1, \ldots, k_l}$ is the cycle type of $\sigma$.

Proposition (order of disjoint cycles) open_in_new

If $\sigma$ is a $(k_1, k_2, \ldots, k_l)$-cycle, then $$o(\sigma) = \mrm{lcm}\set{k_1, k_2, \ldots, k_l}.$$

Definition (adjacent transposition) open_in_new

A transposition $\sigma\in S_n$ in the form $$ \sigma = \pmat{i & i+1} $$ for some $i = 1,2,\ldots, n-1$ is called adjacent.

Lemma (odd adjacent transpositions) open_in_new

Any transposition is a product of an odd number of adjacent transposition.

Proof

Corollary (adjacent transpositions generate) open_in_new

The set of all adjacent transpositions in $S_n$ generate $S_n$.

Proof: transpositions generate, and transpositions can be written as adjacent transpositions.

Definition (inversion) open_in_new

For a permutation $\sigma\in S_n$, a pair $\set{i, j} \subseteq \set{1,2,\ldots,n}$ is called an inversion if $$ i<j \quad\text{but}\quad \sigma(i)>\sigma(j). $$

Lemma (products of even transpositions make identity) open_in_new

Suppose $$\sigma = \tau_1\tau_2\ldots\tau_k$$ where $\tau_1, \tau_2, \ldots, \tau_k$ are all transpositions. If $\sigma = e$, then $k$ is even.

Proof

Theorem (sign homomorphism) open_in_new

The map $$\begin{align*} \sign : && S_n &\to C_2 = \set{-1, 1} \\ &&\tau_1\tau_2\ldots\tau_k &\mapsto (-1)^k \end{align*}$$ where $\tau_1,\ldots,\tau_k$ are transpositions, is a well defined homomorphism.

(Vectors and Matrices use the notation $\epsilon(\sigma)$ instead.)

Proof

Properties of sign of permutation open_in_new

  • $\sign(\rho\sigma) = \sign(\rho)\sign(\sigma)$;

  • $\sign(\rho\inv) = \sign(\rho)$.

Definition (even and odd permutation, alternating group) open_in_new

A permutation $\sigma\in S_n$ is even if $\sign(\sigma) = +1$, and it is odd if $\sign(\sigma) = -1$.

The subgroup of all even permutations $$A_n \equiv \ker(\sign) \lhd S_n$$ is the alternating group on $n$ elements.

Proposition (sign of cycle type) open_in_new

A $(k_1, k_2, \ldots, k_l)$-cycle is even iff the number of even $k_i$ is even.

Proof

Lemma (uniqueness of $A_n$) open_in_new

If $H\leq S_n$ and $|S_n: H| = 2$, then $H=A_n$.

Proof

Theorem (conjugates in S_n iff same cycle type) open_in_new

Two permutations $\sigma_1, \sigma_2\in S_n$ are conjugates iff their cycle types are the same.

Proof

Lemma (conjugacy classes in A_n) open_in_new

Let $\gamma\in A_n\subseteq S_n$

  1. If some odd element of $S_n$ commutes with $\gamma$, then $$\mrm{ccl}_{A_n}(\gamma) = \mrm{ccl}_{S_n}(\gamma).$$

  2. Otherwise i.e. if all elements of $S_n$ that commute with $\gamma$ are even, then the conjugacy class splits into two, $$\mrm{ccl}_{S_n}(\gamma) = \mrm{ccl}_{A_n}(\gamma) \union \mrm{ccl}_{S_n}(\tau\gamma\tau)$$ where $\tau$ is any transposition.

Proof
