First we will check that $n\mathbb Z\leq \mathbb Z$. For all $a=nk, b=nl\in n\mathbb Z$, $$ a-b = nk-nl=n(k-l) \in n\mathbb Z$$ so by the subgroup criterion, $n\mathbb Z\leq\mathbb Z$.

Next, we will prove any subgroup of $\mathbb Z$ is $n\mathbb Z$ for some $n\in\mathbb Z$. Suppose $H\leq \mathbb Z$. If $H=\{0\}$, then $H=0\mathbb Z$.

Otherwise, let $n$ be the smallest positive integer in $H$. $H$ being closed under addition and taking inverses gives $$ kn \in H \quad\text{for all }k\in\mathbb Z, $$ so $n\mathbb Z\leq H$.

Suppose $H\neq n\mathbb Z$, so there exists some $x\in H\setminus n\mathbb Z$. Dividing $x$ by $n$, we have $$ x = qn + r $$ where $q,r\in\mathbb Z$ and $0\leq r < n$. Since $x\neq qn$, $0<r<n$.

However since $x, qn\in H$, $$ r = x-qn \in H, $$ contradicting with $n$ being the smallest positive integer in $H$. Hence $H=n\mathbb Z$.
