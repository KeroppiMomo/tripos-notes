We will prove by induction on $n$.

  • Base case $n=1$: note that $$ O(1) = \set{I, S_1} = \gen{S_1} \isom C_2 $$ where $S_1(x) = -x$ (lol).

  • Inductive step: let $\set{e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_n}$ be the standard basis for $\R^n$. We want to fix $\v e_n$. By setting $v = e_n - Ae_n$, we have $$\begin{align*} S_vAe_n &= Ae_n - \frac{2Ae_n\cdot(e_n - Ae_n)}{(e_n-Ae_n)\cdot(e_n-Ae_n)}(e_n - Ae_n) \\ &= Ae_n - \frac{2Ae_n\cdot e_n - 2Ae_n\cdot Ae_n}{e_n\cdot e_n - 2Ae_n\cdot e_n + Ae_n\cdot Ae_n}(e_n - Ae_n) \\ &= Ae_n - \frac{2Ae_n\cdot e_n - 2}{1-2Ae_n\cdot e_n+1}(e_n-Ae_n) \\ &= Ae_n + e_n - Ae_n \\ &= e_n. \end{align*}$$ So $S_v A$ preserves the plane $P_v$, which is isomorphic to $\R^{n-1}$.

    By induction hypothesis, there exists $w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_{n-1}\in P$ such that $$ S_v A = S_{w_1} S_{w_2} \ldots S_{w_{n-1}} \quad\text{on }P_v.$$ Since both sides of the equation also fix $e_n$, they are equal everywhere in $\R^n$. Therefore $$ A = S_vS_{w_1}S_{w_2} \ldots S_{w_{n-1}}. $$
