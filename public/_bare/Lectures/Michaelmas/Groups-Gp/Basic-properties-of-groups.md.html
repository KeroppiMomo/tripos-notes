Proposition (uniqueness of inverses) open_in_new

Notation (inverse) open_in_new

The inverse of an element $a\in G$ is denoted $a^{-1}$.

This is due to the uniqueness of inverses.

Proposition (inverse of an inverse) open_in_new

For any $a\in G$, $$(a^{-1})^{-1} = a.$$

Proposition (inverse of a product) open_in_new

For any $a,b\in G$, $$ (a\cdot b)^{-1} = b^{-1}\cdot a^{-1}. $$

Notation (power) open_in_new

For any $a\in G$, denote

  • $a^0 = e$;

  • $a^n = a^{n-1}\cdot a$ for all $n=1,2,3,\ldots$;

  • $a^n = (a^{-1})^{-n}$ for all $n=-1,-2,-3,\ldots$.

Proposition (adding exponents) open_in_new

For any $a\in G$ and $m,n\in\mathbb Z$, $$ a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}. $$

Proof

Proposition (multiplying exponents) open_in_new

For any $a\in G$ and $m,n\in\mathbb Z$, $$ (a^m)^n = a^{mn}. $$

Definition (abelian group) open_in_new

If $(G,\cdot,e)$ is a group, and $$a\cdot b=b\cdot a\quad\text{for all }a,b\in G,$$ then $G$ is abelian.

Definition (order of group, finite group) open_in_new

The order of a group $(G,\cdot,e)$ is the number of elements of $G$, denoted by $|G|$.

If $|G|$ is not infinite then $(G,\cdot,e)$ is a finite group.
