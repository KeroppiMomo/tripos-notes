Definition (Riemann sphere) open_in_new

The Riemann sphere is $$ \C_\infty = \C \cup \se{\infty}. $$

Definition (Mobius transformation) open_in_new

Roughly, a Mobius transformation is a map $$ z\mapsto\frac{az+b}{cz+d} $$ for $z\in\C$.

Formal definition: let $a,b,c,d\in\C$ such that $$ ad - bc \neq 0. $$ The corresponding Mobius transformation $$ \mu : \Ci \to \Ci $$ is defined by

  • if $c\neq 0$, $$ z\mapsto\begin{cases} \dfrac{az+b}{cz+d} &\text{if } z\in\C\setminus\se{-d/c}, \\ \infty &\text{if } z = -d/c, \\ \dfrac{a}{c} &\text{if } z=\infty; \end{cases} $$

  • if $c=0$, $$ z\mapsto\begin{cases} \dfrac{az+b}{d} &\text{if } z\in\C, \\ \infty &\text{if } z=\infty. \end{cases}$$

Proposition (composition of Mobius transformation) open_in_new

If $$\mu_1(z) = \frac{a_1z+b_1}{c_1z+d_1},\quad \mu_2(z) = \frac{a_2z+b_2}{c_2z+d_2}, $$ then $$ (\mu_1\circ\mu_2)(z) = \frac{(a_1a_2+b_1c_2)z + (a_1b_2 + b_1d_2)}{(c_1a_2 + d_1c_2)z + (c_1b_2 + d_1d_2)}. $$

Proof

Theorem (Mobius group) open_in_new

Let $\mathcal M$ be the set of all Mobius transformations, $$ \mathcal M = \sb{f: \Ci\to\Ci}{f\text{ is a Mobius transformation}}. $$ Then $(\mathcal M, \circ, \mrm{Id})$ is a group.

Proof

Definition (fixed point) open_in_new

For $f: X\to X$, some $x\in X$ is a fixed point of $f$ if $$ f(x) = x. $$

Lemma (three-point lemma for Mobius transformation) open_in_new

If $\mu\in\mathcal M$ has 3 distinct fixed points, then $\mu = \mrm{Id}_{\Ci}$.

Proof

Proposition (Mobius transformation has at least one fixed point) open_in_new

Every $\mu\in\mathcal M$ has at least 1 fixed point in $\Ci$.

Proof

Lemma (Mobius transformations are sharply triple transitive) open_in_new

For any triples of distinct points $$\begin{align*} z_1,z_2,z_3 &\in \Ci \\ w_1,w_2,w_3 &\in \Ci, \end{align*}$$ there exists a unique $\mu\in\mathcal M$ such that $$ \mu(z_i) = w_i\quad\forall i=1,2,3.$$

Proof

Definition (cross-ratio) open_in_new

Let $z_1, z_2, z_3, z_4\in\Ci$ be distinct. Since $\mathcal M\actson\Ci$ is sharply triply transitive, there exists a unique $\alpha\in\mathcal M$ where $$\begin{align*} \alpha(z_1) &= 0 \\ \alpha(z_2) &= 1 \\ \alpha(z_3) &= \infty. \end{align*}$$ The cross-ratio is defined as $$ [z_1,z_2,z_3,z_4] = \alpha(z_4). $$

In the proof, we have the formula for the cross-ratio, $$ [z_1,z_2,z_3,z_4] = \frac{z_4-z_1}{z_4-z_3}\frac{z_2-z_3}{z_2-z_1}.$$

Proposition (generators of Mobius group) open_in_new

$\mathcal M$ is generated by the set of elements in the following three forms:

  • $\alpha_a: z\mapsto az$ where $a\in\C\setminus\se 0$;

  • $\beta_b: z\mapsto z+b$ where $b\in\C$;

  • $\gamma: z\mapsto 1/z$.

Defintion (circle in Riemann sphere) open_in_new

A circle in $\Ci$ is either

  • an Euclidean circle in $\C$, which is defined by $$ \sb{z\in\C}{|z-c| = r} $$ where $c\in\C$ and $r>0$; or

  • an Euclidean straight line with the point $\infty$, defined by $$ \sb{z\in\C}{|z-a| = |z-b|} \cup \set\infty $$ where $a,b\in\C$. (This is the perpendicular bisector of $a,b$.)

Theorem (Mobius transformations preserve circles) open_in_new

If $C\subseteq\C$ is a circle and $\mu\in\mathcal M$, then $\mu(C)$ is also a circle.

Proof
