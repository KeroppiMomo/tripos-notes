Introduction of Groups open_in_new

Definition (binary operation) open_in_new

A binary operation $\cdot$ of a set $X$ is a map $X\times X\to X$, $$ (x,y) \mapsto x\cdot y. $$

Definition (group) open_in_new

A group is a tuple $(G, \cdot, e)$ where

  • $G$ is a set;

  • $\cdot$ is a binary operation of $G$; and

  • $e \in G$,

satisfying the following three axioms:

  • Associativity: for all $a,b,c \in G$, $$ (a+b)+c = a+(b+c);$$

  • Identity: for all $g \in G$, $$ g\cdot e = g. $$

  • Inverses: for all $g\in G$, there exists $h\in G$, called an inverse of $g$, such that $$g\cdot h = e.$$

Note. "Closure" is included in the definition of binary operation.

Subgroups open_in_new

Definition (subgroup) open_in_new

Let $(G, \cdot_G, e_G)$ and $(H, \cdot_H, e_H)$ be groups. If

  • $H\subseteq G$;

  • $e_H = e_G$; and

  • $a\cdot_H b = a\cdot_G b$ for all $a,b\in H$,

then $H$ is a subgroup of $G$, denoted as $H\leq G$.

Proposition (subgroup criterion) open_in_new

Let $G$ be a group and $H\subseteq G$.

If $G\neq\emptyset$ and $$ a\cdot b^{-1}\in H \quad\text{for all }a,b\in H,$$ then $H\leq G$.

Proof

Proposition (intersection of subgroups is a subgroup) open_in_new

For a group $G$, if $H_i\leq G$ for some set of $i$, then $$ H = \bigcap_i H_i \leq G. $$

Proof

Definition (generating set) open_in_new

For any subset $X\subseteq G$, define $$ \langle X\rangle = \bigcup_{X\subseteq H\leq G} H$$ be the subgroup generated by X.

If $\langle X\rangle = G$, then we say $X$ generates $G$.

$\langle X\rangle\leq G$ because $\langle X\rangle$ is the intersection of subgroups.

Examples of subgroups open_in_new

  • For every group $G$, $1_G = \{ e_G \}\leq G$ is called the trivial subgroup.

Other groups are called proper subgroups.

  • $\mathbb Z\leq\mathbb Q\leq\mathbb R\leq\mathbb C$.

Definition (dihedral group) open_in_new

Let $X_n\subseteq\mathbb C$ be the vertices of an $n$-gon $$ X_n = \{e^{2\pi ik/n}\mid k=0,1,2,\ldots,n-1\}$$ where $n\geq 3$.

Then $|\mathrm D_{2n}| = 2n$ and $$ \mathrm D_{2n} = \{e,r,r^2,\ldots, r^{n-1},s,rs,r^2s, \ldots, r^{n-1}s\}. $$

Lemmas and proof

Lemma (dihedral relation) open_in_new

For $r,s\in\mathrm D_{2n}$ defined above, we have $$ sr = r^{-1}s. $$

Proof

Using this relation, algebra becomes easy, e.g. $$\begin{align*} r^k s r^l &= r^k s r r^{l-1} \\ &= r^k r^{-1} s r^{l-1} \\ &= \ldots \\ &= r^{k-l} s \end{align*}$$

Definition (homomorphism) open_in_new

A map between groups $$ \phi : G\to H $$ is a homomorphism if $$ \phi(g\cdot g') = \phi(g)\cdot\phi(g')\quad \forall g,g'\in G. $$

Lemma (homomorphism sends identity to identity) open_in_new

If $\phi: G\to H$ is a homomorphism, then $$\phi(e_G) = e_H.$$

Proof

Lemma (homomorphism preserves inverses) open_in_new

If $\phi: G\to H$ is a homomorphism, then $$ \phi(g^{-1}) = (\phi(g))^{-1} \quad\forall g\in G. $$

Proof

Lemma (composition of homomorphisms) open_in_new

If $G\xrightarrow{\phi} H\xrightarrow{\psi} K$ are homomorphisms, so is their composition $\psi\circ\phi$.

Proof

Proposition (image on generating set determines homomorphism) open_in_new

If $\phi: G \to H$ be a homomorphism and $G = \gen X$, then $\phi$ is uniquely determined by the image $\phi(X)$. (Example sheet 1, Q9)

For any group $G,H$, a map $$\begin{align*} \phi: G&\to H \\ g&\mapsto e_H \end{align*}$$ is the trivial homomorphism.

Image and kernel open_in_new

Definition (image and kernel of homomorphism) open_in_new

Let $\phi: G\to H$ be a homomorphism. Its image is defined as $$ \mathrm{im}(\phi) = \{\phi(g)\in H\mid g\in G\}. $$ Its kernel is defined as $$ \mathrm{ker}(\phi) = \{g\in G\mid \phi(g) = e_H\}. $$

Proposition (image and kernel are subgroups) open_in_new

If $\phi: G\to H$ is a homomorphism, then

  1. $\mathrm{im}\,\phi \leq H$;

  2. $\mathrm{ker}\,\phi \leq G$.

Proof

Proposition (isomorphism, image and kernel) open_in_new

Let $\phi: G\to H$ be a homomorphism.

  1. $\phi$ is surjective iff $\im\phi = H$.

  2. $\phi$ is injective iff $\ker\phi = 1_G = \{e_G\}$ is the trivial subgroup of $G$.

  3. $\phi$ is bijective iff $\im\phi = H$ and $\ker\phi = 1_G$.

Proof

Group actions open_in_new

Definition (group action) open_in_new

An action of a group $G$ on a set $X$ is a map $$\begin{align*} G\times X &\to X \\ (g, x) &\mapsto gx\text{ or } g(x) \end{align*}$$ such that

  • $ex = x$ for all $x\in X$;

  • $(gh)x = g(hx)$ for all $g,h\in G$ and $x\in X$.

We write $G\curvearrowright X$ to indicate that $G$ acts on $X$.

Examples of actions open_in_new

  • For any group $G$ and a set $X$, $$ gx = x $$ for all $g\in G, x\in X$ is the trivial action.

  • The symmetry group $\mathrm{Sym}(X)$ acts on $X$ via $$ fx = f(x) $$ for all $f\in\mathrm{Sym}(X)$ and $x\in X$.

  • For any $X\subseteq\C$, $\mathrm{Isom}(X) \leq \mathrm{Sym}(X)$ acts on $X$.

  • Every group $G$ acts on itself, i.e. $X=G$, via $$ g\gamma = g\cdot\gamma$$ for all $g\in G, \gamma\in X=G$. This is called the regular action.

Orbits and stabiliser open_in_new

Definition (orbits and stabilisers) open_in_new

Let $G\curvearrowright X$ and let $x\in X$.

  • The orbit of $x$ is the set $$ Gx = \sb{gx}{g\in G}. $$

  • The stabiliser of $x$ is the set $$ \mathrm{Stab}_G (x) = \sb{g\in G}{gx = x}. $$

Definition (transitive action) open_in_new

$G\actson X$ is called transitive when: (equivalent definitions)

  • $Gx = X$ for all $x\in X$;

  • for all $x,y\in X$, there exists $g\in G$ such that $gx = y$.

Definition (faithful action) open_in_new

$G \actson X$ is faithful when: (equivalent definitions)

  • there does not exist $g\in G\setminus\se{e}$ such that $gx = x$ for all $x\in X$;

  • for all $g\in G\setminus\se{e}$, there exists $x\in X$ where $gx \neq x$;

  • the associated homomorphism $G\to\mrm{Sym}(X)$ is injective.

Proposition (stabilisers form a subgroup) open_in_new

Suppose $G\actson X$. Then for all $x\in X$, $\mrm{Stab}_G(x) \leq G$.

Proof

Proposition (orbits partition) open_in_new

Suppose $G\actson X$. The set of all orbits $$ \sb{Gy}{y\in X} $$ form a partition of $X$, i.e. every $x\in X$ is in exactly one orbit.

Proof

For any subgroup $H\leq G$, the right cosets $H\setminus G$ partition $G$.

Proposition (left coset as orbit of action) open_in_new

Proof

Conjugation open_in_new

Definition (conjugation) open_in_new

Let $G$ be a group and $g\in G$. For all $h\in G$, the element $$ hgh^{-1} \in G $$ is called the conjugate of $g$ by $h$.

Examples of conjugations open_in_new

  • If $G$ is abelian, then for any $g,h\in G$, $$ hgh^{-1} = hh^{-1} g = g $$ so the only conjugate of $g$ is $g$ itself.

  • In $D_{2n}$, consider the conjugate of $s$ by $r$. By the dihedral relation, $$\begin{align*} r^{-1} s &= sr \\ r^2r^{-1} s r^{-1} &= r^2 s r r^{-1}\\ rsr^{-1} &= r^2 s \end{align*}$$ More generally, $$ r^k s r^{-k} = r^{2k} s. $$ What reflection is this? Note that $$\begin{align*} (r^{2k} s)(e^{2\pi ik/n}) &= (r^ksr^{-k})(e^{2\pi ik/n}) \\ &= (r^k s)(1) \\ &= r^k(1) \\ &= e^{2\pi ik/n} \end{align*}$$ so $r^{2k} s$ fixes $e^{2\pi ik/n}$ and therefore reflects about the line of symmetry through $e^{2\pi ik/n}$.

Proposition (conjugation as an action) open_in_new

$G$ acts on $G$ by conjugation: $$ g(\gamma) = g\gamma g^{-1}. $$

Proof

Definition (conjugacy class, centraliser) open_in_new

  • The orbit of $\gamma$ under the conjugation action is called the conjugacy class of $\gamma$, $$ \mrm{ccl}(\gamma) = \sb{g\gamma g^{-1}}{g\in G}. $$ This is the set of all conjugates of $\gamma$.

  • The stabiliser of $\gamma$ under the conjugation action is called the centraliser of $\gamma$, $$ C_G(\gamma) = \sb{g\in G}{g\gamma g^{-1} = \gamma}. $$ This is set of all elements in $G$ that commute with $\gamma$, i.e. $g\gamma = \gamma g$.

Definition (centre of a group) open_in_new

The centre of a group $G$ is the set of elements that commute with all elements in $G$, $$ Z(G) = \sb{h\in G}{gh = hg\ \forall g\in G} = \bigcap_{g\in G}C_G(g).$$

The centre is precisely the union of the conjugacy classes of size 1.

The Möbius Group open_in_new

Definition (Riemann sphere) open_in_new

The Riemann sphere is $$ \C_\infty = \C \cup \se{\infty}. $$

Definition (Mobius transformation) open_in_new

Roughly, a Mobius transformation is a map $$ z\mapsto\frac{az+b}{cz+d} $$ for $z\in\C$.

Formal definition: let $a,b,c,d\in\C$ such that $$ ad - bc \neq 0. $$ The corresponding Mobius transformation $$ \mu : \Ci \to \Ci $$ is defined by

  • if $c\neq 0$, $$ z\mapsto\begin{cases} \dfrac{az+b}{cz+d} &\text{if } z\in\C\setminus\se{-d/c}, \\ \infty &\text{if } z = -d/c, \\ \dfrac{a}{c} &\text{if } z=\infty; \end{cases} $$

  • if $c=0$, $$ z\mapsto\begin{cases} \dfrac{az+b}{d} &\text{if } z\in\C, \\ \infty &\text{if } z=\infty. \end{cases}$$

Proposition (composition of Mobius transformation) open_in_new

If $$\mu_1(z) = \frac{a_1z+b_1}{c_1z+d_1},\quad \mu_2(z) = \frac{a_2z+b_2}{c_2z+d_2}, $$ then $$ (\mu_1\circ\mu_2)(z) = \frac{(a_1a_2+b_1c_2)z + (a_1b_2 + b_1d_2)}{(c_1a_2 + d_1c_2)z + (c_1b_2 + d_1d_2)}. $$

Proof

Theorem (Mobius group) open_in_new

Let $\mathcal M$ be the set of all Mobius transformations, $$ \mathcal M = \sb{f: \Ci\to\Ci}{f\text{ is a Mobius transformation}}. $$ Then $(\mathcal M, \circ, \mrm{Id})$ is a group.

Proof

Definition (cross-ratio) open_in_new

Let $z_1, z_2, z_3, z_4\in\Ci$ be distinct. Since $\mathcal M\actson\Ci$ is sharply triply transitive, there exists a unique $\alpha\in\mathcal M$ where $$\begin{align*} \alpha(z_1) &= 0 \\ \alpha(z_2) &= 1 \\ \alpha(z_3) &= \infty. \end{align*}$$ The cross-ratio is defined as $$ [z_1,z_2,z_3,z_4] = \alpha(z_4). $$

Definition (direct product of groups) open_in_new

If $G,H$ are groups, their direct product is $$ G\times H = \sb{(g,h)}{g\in G,h\in H} $$ with operation $$ (g_1, h_1)\cdot(g_2,h_2) = (g_1 \cdot_G g_2, h_1\cdot_H h_2). $$

$G\times H$ is a group with identity $(e_G, e_H)$ and inverse $(g,h)\inv = (g\inv, h\inv)$. It has order $|G\times H| = |G||H|$.

Theorem (direct product theorem) open_in_new

If $H_1,H_2\leq G$ satisfy

  1. $H_1\cap H_2 = \set e$;

  2. $h_1 h_2 = h_2 h_1$ for all $h_1\in H_1, h_2\in H_2$; and

  3. for all $g\in G$, we have $g = h_1 h_2$ for some $h_1\in H_1, h_2\in H_2$,

then $G \cong H_1\times H_2$.

Proof

Normal subgroups open_in_new

Definition (normal subgroup) open_in_new

A subgroup $H\leq G$ is called normal if $$ ghg\inv \in H \quad \forall h\in H, g\in G. $$ We denote this as $H\lhd G$.

Example of normal subgroups open_in_new

  • For all group $G$, $1\lhd G$ and $G\lhd G$.

  • If $G$ is abelian, then any $H\leq G$ is normal.

  • $\gen r\lhd D_6$ since $$\begin{align*} srs\inv = r\inv ss\inv = r^2 &\in \gen r \\ sr^2s\inv =r^{-2} ss\inv = r &\in \gen r \end{align*}$$ etc, but $\gen s$ is not normal since $$ rsr\inv = sr^{-2} = sr \not\in \gen s. $$

Proposition (kernel is a normal subgroup) open_in_new

Suppose $\phi: G\to K$ is a homomorphism. Then $\ker\phi\lhd G$.

Proof

Lemma (left and right cosets equal iff normal subgroup) open_in_new

Suppose $H\leq G$. Then $H\lhd G$ iff $gH = Hg$ for all $g\in G$.

Proof

Proposition (normal subgroup is a union of conjugacy classes) open_in_new

If $H \lhd G$, then $H$ is the union of $\ccl_{G}(\gamma)$ for some elements $\gamma\in G$.

Theorem (quotient groups) open_in_new

If $H\lhd G$, then the set of (left) cosets $G/H$ is a group with operation $$ (g_1 H)(g_2 H) = (g_1 g_2) H.$$

Proof

Definition (quotient group) open_in_new

If $H\lhd G$, then $G/H$ is called the quotient of $G$ by $H$.

Examples of quotients open_in_new

  • $G/1\isom G$ and $G/G \isom 1$.

  • Since $\Z$ is abelian, any subgroup $n\Z \lhd \Z$. Also the quotient $\Z/n\Z$ is generated by $1+n\Z$, so we have $\Z/n\Z \isom C_n$.

  • Proposition (subgroups of index 2) open_in_new

    Suppose $H\leq G$. If $|G:H| = 2$, then $H\lhd G$ and $G/H \isom C_2$.

    Proof (subgroups of index 2) open_in_new

    If $|G:H| = 2$, then for any $g\in G\setminus H$, $$ gH = G \setminus H = Hg $$ so $H\lhd G$. Also since $|G/H|=2$, we know that $G/H \isom C_2$.

  • $C_n = \gen r\leq D_{2n}$ and $|D_{2n} : \gen r| = 2$, so $\gen r\lhd D_{2n}$ and $D_{2n} / C_{2n} \isom C_2$.

  • Quotients are not subgroups!

  • Also you cannot "multiply" quotients, e.g. $C_4 / C_2 \isom C_2$ and $K_4/C_2\isom C_2$ but $C_4\not\isom K_4$.

Theorem (isomorphism theorem) open_in_new

If $\phi: G\to H$ is a homomorphism, then $$ \ker\phi \lhd G \quad \text{and} \quad G / \ker\phi \isom \im\phi. $$

Proof

Examples using isomorphism theorem open_in_new

  • $$\begin{align*} \phi: \Z&\to C_n \\ k&\mapsto e^{2\pi ik/n} \end{align*}$$ is a homomorphism with $\ker\phi = n\Z$ and $\im\phi = C_n$, so we have $\Z/n\Z \isom C_n$.

  • $$\begin{align*} \phi: \R&\to\C_\times \\ t&\mapsto e^{2\pi it} \end{align*}$$ is a homomorphism with $\ker\phi = \Z$ and $\im\phi = \sb{z\in\C}{|z| = 1} \equiv \mathbb S^1$, so we have $\R/\Z = \mathbb S^1$.

Permutations open_in_new

Definition (permutation) open_in_new

A permutation is a bijective function $f: X\to X$. This means that $f$ has an inverse $f^{-1}:X\to X$ where $$ f\circ f^{-1} = Id_X = f^{-1}\circ f. $$

Definition (symmetric groups) open_in_new

$\mathrm{Sym}(X)$ is defined to be the set of all permutations of $X$.

Notation (permutation) open_in_new

For $\rho\in S_n$, we use the notation $$ \rho = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 & \ldots & n \\ \rho(1) & \rho(2) & \rho(3) & \ldots & \rho(n) \end{pmatrix}. $$

We often omit fixed points, i.e. $k$ such that $\rho(k) = k$.

Disjoint cycles open_in_new

Definition (q-cycle) open_in_new

A permutation $\mu\in S_n$ is a $q$-cycle if for some distinct $a_1,\ldots,a_q = 1,2,\ldots, n$, $$ \mu = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & \ldots & a_{q-1} & a_q \\ a_2 & a_3 & \ldots & a_q & a_1 \end{pmatrix}.$$ We denote this as $$ \mu = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & \ldots & a_q \end{pmatrix}. $$ A 2-cycle is called a transposition.

Definition (disjoint cycles) open_in_new

Two cycles $\pmat{a_1 & \ldots & a_k}$ and $\pmat{b_1 & \ldots & b_l}$ are called disjoint if $a_i \neq b_j$ for all $i,j$.

Definition (disjoint permutation) open_in_new

Two permutations $\mu,\nu\in S_n$ are disjoint when for all $x=1,2,\ldots,n$, if $\mu(x) \neq x$, then $\nu(x) = x$, and similarly if $\nu(x) \neq x$, then $\mu(x) = x$.

Proposition (disjoint permutations commute) open_in_new

For two permutations $\mu,\nu\in S_n$, if they are disjoint, then $$ \mu\circ\nu = \nu\circ\mu. $$

Proof

Theorem (disjoint cycles) open_in_new

Every $\sigma\in S_n$ can be written as a product of disjoint cycles.

The expression is unique up to

  • switching the elements of each cycle; and

  • permuting the cycles.

Proof

Definition (cycle type) open_in_new

If $\sigma = \pmat{a_1^1 & a_2^1 & \ldots & a_{k_1}^1} \ldots \pmat{a_1^l & a_2^l & \ldots & a_{k_l}^l}$ are disjoint cycles, then $\sigma$ is called a $(k_1, k_2, \ldots, k_l)$-cycle. The set of numbers $\set{k_1, \ldots, k_l}$ is the cycle type of $\sigma$.

Proposition (order of disjoint cycles) open_in_new

If $\sigma$ is a $(k_1, k_2, \ldots, k_l)$-cycle, then $$o(\sigma) = \mrm{lcm}\set{k_1, k_2, \ldots, k_l}.$$

Matrices open_in_new

Notation (set of matrices) open_in_new

Denote $M_n(F)$ be the set of all $n\times n$ matrices with entries in $F$, where $F=\R$ or $\C$.

Definition (general linear group) open_in_new

Define the general linear group be $$\mathrm{GL}_n(\R) = \sb{A\in M_n(\R)}{\det A \neq 0}.$$ Same for $\C$. This is a group under multiplication.

Determinant as homomorphism open_in_new

The map $$\begin{align*} \mathrm{GL_n}(\mathbb R) &\to \R_\times = (\mathbb R\setminus \{0\},\times,1) \\ A &\mapsto \det A \end{align*}$$ is a homomorphism since $$ \det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)$$

Definition (special linear group) open_in_new

The special linear group is the set of all matrices with determinant 1: $$ \mrm{SL}_n(F) = \ker(\det) = \sb{A\in\mrm{GL}_n(F)}{\det A = 1}. $$

Quotient of general linear group by special linear group open_in_new

By the isomorphism theorem, $\sl n\R \lhd \gl n\R$ and $$ \gl n\R / \sl n\R \isom \im(\det) = \R_\times $$ since $xI \in \gl n\R$ and $\det(xI) = x$ for all $x\in\R_\times$.

Same for $\C$.

Proposition (orbit under matrix conjugation action equals matrix in any basis) open_in_new

Consider the action $\gl n\R \actson M_n(\R)$ by matrix conjugation $$ (P, A) \mapsto PAP\inv. $$

Let $V$ be an $n$-dimensional vector space and let $\alpha: V\to V$ be a linear map. If $A \in M_n(\R)$ represents $\alpha$ in some basis, then the orbit $$ \gl n\R A = \sb{PAP\inv}{P\in\gl n\R} $$ equals the set of all matrices that represent $\alpha$ in any basis.

Proof

Proposition (Mobius group as quotient) open_in_new

Identify $\C_\times = \sb{\pmat{\lambda&0\\0&\lambda}\in\gl 2\C}{\lambda\in\C\setminus\set 0}$. Then $$ \C_\times \lhd \gl 2\C \quad \text{ and } \quad \gl 2\C / \C_\times = \mathcal M. $$

Proof

Orthogonal groups open_in_new

Definition (orthogonal groups) open_in_new

The $n$-dimensional orthogonal group is the subgroup of $\gl n\R$ that preserves distance in $\R^n$: $$ O(n) = \sb{A\in\gl n\R}{\|Av\| = \|v\|\ \forall v\in\R^n}. $$

Lemma (equivalent definitions of orthogonal matrices) open_in_new

Let $P\in M_n(\R)$. The following are equivalent:

  1. $P^T P = I$.

  2. $|P\v x| = |\v x|$ for all $\v x\in\R^n$;

  3. $(P\v x)\cdot(P\v y) = \v x\cdot\v y$ for all $\v x,\v y\in\R^n$.

  4. if $(\v v_1, \v v_2, \ldots, \v v_n)$ are orthonormal, then so are $(P\v v_1, P\v v_2, \ldots, P\v v_n)$;

  5. columns of $P$ form an orthonormal basis.

Proof

Proposition (determinant of orthogonal and unitary matrix) open_in_new

  • If $A$ is orthogonal, then $\det A = \pm 1$ (Proof). In particular, in $\R^3$, $A$ represents a rotation ($\det A = 1$) or a reflection ($\det A = -1$).

  • If $U$ is unitary, then $|\det U| = 1$ (Proof).

Definition (special orthogonal group) open_in_new

The special orthogonal group is the group of all orthogonal matrices with determinant 1, $$ \mrm{SO}(n) = O(n) \inter \sl n\R = \sb{A\in O(n)}{\det A=1}.$$

Definition (reflection) open_in_new

Any $v\in\R^n \setminus \set 0$ define an orthogonal hyperplane $P_v = \sb{x\in R^n}{x\cdot v = 0}.$ The reflection in $P_v$ is defined to be $$ S_v(x) = x - \frac{2(x\cdot v)}{\|v\|^2}v. $$

Properties of reflection open_in_new

  • We can assume $v$ is normalised to $v/\|v\|$.

  • $S_v^2 = I$. (Proof)

  • $S_v \in O(n)$. (Proof)

  • $\det S_v = -1$, so $S_v\not\in \mrm{SO}(n)$. (Proof)

Theorem (reflection generate $O(n)$) open_in_new

Every $A\in O(n)$ is a product of at most $n$ reflections.

Proof

Lemma (elements of $O(2)$) open_in_new

Let $A\in O(2)$.

  • If $A\not\in \mrm{SO}(2)$, then $A$ is a reflection;

  • If $A\in\mrm{SO}(2)$, then $A$ is a rotation, here means that $A=I$ or $\mrm{Fix}(A) = \set 0$.

Proof

Lemma ($SO(3)$ fixes a line) open_in_new

If $A\in\mrm{SO}(3)$, then $A$ is a rotation, here defined as $A=I$ or $\mrm{Fix}(A)$ is a line.

Platonic solids open_in_new

Definition (Platonic solid) open_in_new

A convex polyhedra $X\subseteq \R^3$ is a Platonic solid if

  • every face is a regular $n$-gon for some $n$;

  • $G=\mrm{Isom}(X)$ acts transitively on the faces;

  • if $x\in X$ is the midpoint of a face, then $\mrm{Stab}_G(x)\isom D_{2n}$.

Theorem (all Platonic solids) open_in_new

There are, up to similarity, 5 Platonic solids:

  • the tetrahedron (4 triangular faces, 4 vertices);

  • the cube (hexahedron) (6 square faces, 8 vertices);

  • the octahedron (8 triangular faces, 6 vertices);

  • the dodecahedron (12 pentagonal faces, 20 vertices);

  • the icosahedron (20 triangular faces, 12 vertices).

Dual polyhedra open_in_new

Two solids $x,y$ are dual if $y$ can be constructed by putting vertices in the centres of the faces of $x$ and then joining the vertices in adjacent faces by edges.

For the Platonic solids:

  • the tetrahedron is dual with itself;

  • the cube and the octahedron are dual; and

  • the dodecahedron and the icosahedron are dual.

If $X$ and $Y$ are dual, then $\mrm{Isom}(X)\isom\mrm{Isom}(Y)$.
