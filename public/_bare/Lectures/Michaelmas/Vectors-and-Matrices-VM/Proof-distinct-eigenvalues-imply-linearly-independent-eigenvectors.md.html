We will prove by contradiction. Suppose $\v x_1, \v x_2, \ldots, \v x_n$ are linearly dependent.

Then there exists a smallest subset $\set{\v x_{J_1}, \v x_{J_2}, \ldots, \v x_{J_r}} \subseteq \set{\v x_1, \v x_2, \ldots, \v x_n}$, i.e. with minimum $r$, with a non-trivial linear combination $$ d_1 \v x_{J_1} + d_2 \v x_{J_2} + \ldots + d_r \v x_{J_r} = \v 0$$ where $d_1, \ldots, d_r$ are scalars not all zero.

Then applying $A - \lambda_{J_1} I$ to the equation, $$\begin{align*} \v 0 &= d_1 (A\v x_{J_1} - \lambda_{J_1}\v x_{J_1}) + d_2(A\v x_{J_2} - \lambda_{J_1}\v x_{J_2}) + \ldots + d_r (A\v x_{J_r} - \lambda_{J_1}\v x_{J_r}) \\ &= d_1(\lambda_{J_1} - \lambda_{J_1})\v x_{J_1} + d_2(\lambda_{J_2} - \lambda_{J_1})\v x_{J_2} + \ldots + d_r(\lambda_{J_r} - \lambda_{J_1})\v x_{J_r} \end{align*}$$ and only the first term is 0 since $\lambda_{J_1}, \lambda_{J_2}, \ldots, \lambda_{J_r}$ are distinct.

This makes the a shorter linear combination of the linearly dependent vectors, which is a contradiction.
