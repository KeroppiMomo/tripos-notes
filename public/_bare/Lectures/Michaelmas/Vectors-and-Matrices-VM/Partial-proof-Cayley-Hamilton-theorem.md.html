We will prove for the case when $A$ is a diagonalisable matrix, i.e. $$ A = P\inv DP $$ where $$ D = \mrm{diag}(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_n) $$ and $\lambda_j$ are eigenvalues.

Note that $$\begin{align*} A^i &= \ub{P\inv D PP\inv D P\ldots P\inv DP}_{i\text{ times}} \\ &= P\inv D^i P \\ &= P\inv \mrm{diag}(\lambda_1^i, \lambda_2^i, \ldots, \lambda_n^i) P. \end{align*}$$

Therefore $$\begin{align*} p_A(A) &= P\inv p_A(D) P \\ &= P\inv \mrm{diag}(p_A(\lambda_1), p_A(\lambda_2), \ldots, p_A(\lambda_n))P \\ &= P\inv 0 P \\ &= 0. \end{align*}$$

For the case when $A$ is $2\times 2$ but not diagonalisable, $A$ is similar to $$ B = \pmat{\lambda&1 \\ 0&\lambda}. $$ Now similar matrices have the same characteristic polynomials, so $$ p_A(z) = p_B(z) = \det(B - zI) = (\lambda - z)^2. $$

Therefore $$\begin{align*} p_A(A) &= p_A(P\inv BP) \\ &= P\inv p_A(B) P \\ &= P\inv (\lambda I - B)^2 P \\ &= P\inv \pmat{0&-1\\0&0}^2 P \\ &= 0. \end{align*}$$
