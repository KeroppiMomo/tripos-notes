Let $I$ be any integer from 1 to $n$. For any permutation $\mu:i\mapsto J_i$, define $\sigma\in S_n$ be the permutation which maps $I$ to $J_I$ and keeps other elements in their natural order, i.e. $$ \sigma = \begin{cases} \pmat{1 & \ldots & I-1 & I & I+1 & \ldots & J_I & J_I+1 & \ldots & n \\ 1 & \ldots & I-1 & \color{yellow} J_I & \color{yellow} I & \color{yellow} \ldots & \color{yellow} J_I-1 & J_I+1 & \ldots & n }, & J_I > I \\ \pmat{1 & \ldots & J_I-1 & J_I & \ldots & I-1 & I & I+1 & \ldots & n \\ 1 & \ldots & J_I-1 & \color{yellow} J_I+1 & \color{yellow} \ldots & \color{yellow} I & \color{yellow} J_I & I+1 & \ldots & n }, & J_I < I \\ \mrm{Id}, & J_I = I. \end{cases} $$ There are $|I-J_I|$ transpositions in $\sigma$, so $$ \epsilon(\sigma) = (-1)^{I-J_I}. $$

Next define $\rho\in S_n$ be the permutation where the top row going from 1 to $n$ misses out $J_I$ and the bottom row going from $J_1$ to $J_n$ also misses out $J_I$, i.e. using $\color{red}\overline{\quad}$ to denote missing values, $$ \rho = \begin{cases} \pmat{1 & \ldots & I-1 & & I & \ldots & J_I-1 & \color{red} \overline{J_I} & J_I+1 & \ldots & n \\ J_1 & \ldots & J_{I-1} & \color{red} \overline{J_I} & \color{yellow} J_{I+1} & \color{yellow} \ldots & \color{yellow} J_{J_I} & & J_{J_I+1} & \ldots & J_n }, & J_I > I \\ \pmat{1 & \ldots & J_I-1 & \color{red}\overline{J_I} & J_{I}+1 & \ldots & I & & I+1 & \ldots & n \\ J_1 & \ldots & J_{J_I-1} & & \color{yellow} J_{J_I} & \color{yellow} \ldots & \color{yellow} J_{I-1} & \color{red}\overline{J_n} & J_{J_I+1} & \ldots & J_n }, & J_I < I \\ \pmat{1 & \ldots & I - 1 & \color{red}\overline{I} & I + 1 & \ldots & n \\ J_1 & \ldots & J_{I - 1} & \color{red}\overline{J_I} & J_{I+1} & \ldots & J_n}, & J_I = I. \end{cases} $$ Since $\rho$ is just a permutation of $J_1, \ldots, {\color{red}\overline{J_I}}, \ldots, J_n$, $$ \epsilon(\rho) = \epsilon_{J_1 J_2 \ldots{\color{red}\overline{J_I}}\ldots J_n}. $$

By checking case by case, we can see that $$ \mu = \rho \sigma, $$ so $$ \epsilon_{J_1J_2\ldots J_n} = \epsilon(\mu) = \epsilon(\rho)\epsilon(\sigma) = (-1)^{I-J_I} \epsilon_{J_1J_2\ldots{\color{red}\overline{J_I}}\ldots J_n}.$$

Finally, we can write the determinant of $A$ as $$\begin{align*} \det A& = \sum_{J_1,J_2,\ldots,J_n=1}^n \epsilon_{J_1J_2\ldots J_n} A_{J_1 1} A_{J_2 2} \ldots A_{J_n n} \\ &= \sum_{J_I=1}^n A_{I} \sum_{J_1,J_2,\ldots,{\color{red}\overline{J_I}},\ldots J_n} \epsilon_{J_1J_2\ldots J_I \ldots J_n} A_{J_1 1} A_{J_2 2} \ldots {\color{red}\overline{A_{J_I I}}}\ldots A_{J_n n} \\ &= \sum_{J_I=I}^n A_I (-1)^{I-J_I} \sum_{J_1,J_2,\ldots,{\color{red}\overline{J_I}},\ldots J_n} \epsilon_{J_1J_2\ldots {\color{red}\overline{J_I}} \ldots J_n} A_{J_1 1} A_{J_2 2} \ldots {\color{red}\overline{A_{J_I I}}}\ldots A_{J_n n} \\ &= \sum_{J_I=1}^n A_I(-1)^{I-J_I} M_{J_I I} \\ &= \sum_{J_I=1}^n A_I \Delta_{J_I I} \end{align*}$$ as required.

For expanding along a row, use $\det A^T$.
