Hermitian matrix (non-examinable)

Let $\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_r$ where $r\leq n$ be the distinct eigenvalues of $H$ with the corresponding orthogonal eigenvectors $B = \set{\v v_1, \v v_2, \ldots, \v v_r}$.

Spoiler alert, we are possibly going to repeat the following process, so let us set some base cases.

  • If $r=n$, then $B$ already form an orthogonal basis so we are done.

  • $r\geq 1$ since by the fundamental theorem of algebra.

We can now assume $1\leq r<n$. We can also assume $B$ is an orthonormal set by normalising the eigenvectors.

Extend $B$ to a basis of the whole of $\C^n$ $$ \set{\ub{\v v_1, \v v_2, \ldots, \v v_r}_\text{orthonormal}, \v w_1, \v w_2, \ldots, \v w_{n-r}}, $$ and then perform Gram-Schmidt process to form an orthonormal basis $$ \set{\ub{\v v_1, \v v_2, \ldots, \v v_r}_\text{unchanged}, \v u_1, \v u_2, \ldots, \v u_{n-r}}. $$

We now change the basis of $H$ to this basis. The first $r$ columns are all 0 except on the diagonal since $$ H\v v_j = \lambda_k\v v_j. $$ For the last $(n-r)$ columns, we can write the coefficient of $\v v_i$ in $H\v u_j$ expressed as a linear combination of the basis vectors as $\v v_i^\dagger H\v u_j$ since the basis is orthonormal. To see that this is zero, take the Hermitian conjugate: $$\begin{align*} (\v v_i^\dagger H\v u_j)^\dagger &= \v u_j^\dagger H^\dagger \v v_i \\ &= \v u_j^\dagger H\v v_i \\ &= \lambda_i\v u_j^\dagger \v v_i &&\text{$\v v_i$ is an eigenvector} \\ &= 0 &&\text{$\v u_j$ and $\v v_i$ are orthonormal}. \end{align*}$$ Hence $H$ after the change of basis is $$\begin{align*} P\inv HP &= \pmat{ \lambda_1 &0 &\ldots & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda_2 & \ldots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \ldots & \lambda_r \\ &&&& c_{11} & c_{12} & \ldots & c_{1,n-r} \\ &&&& c_{21} & c_{22} & \ldots & c_{2,n-r} \\ &&&& \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ &&&& c_{(n-r)1} & c_{(n-r)2} & \ldots & c_{(n-r)(n-r)} } \\ &\equiv\pmat{\mrm{diag}(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\ldots,\lambda_r) & 0 \\ 0 & C}. \end{align*}$$ $C$ is not $0\times 0$ since we assumed $r<n$. Note that since we changed between orthonormal bases, $P$ is unitary.

Now to show that $C$ is Hermitian, notice $$\begin{align*} (P\inv HP)^\dagger &= (P^\dagger HP)^\dagger &&\text{$P$ is unitary} \\ &= P^\dagger H^\dagger P \\ &= P^\dagger HP &&\text{$H$ is Hermitian} \\ &= P\inv HP \\ \pmat{\mrm{diag}(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n) & 0 \\ 0 & C^\dagger} &= \pmat{\mrm{diag}(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n) & 0 \\ 0 & C} \\ C^\dagger &= C. \end{align*}$$

Also the eigenvalues of $C$ are eigenvalues of $H$ because $$ \det(H - \lambda I) = \det(P\inv HP-\lambda I) = (\lambda_1-\lambda)\ldots(\lambda_r-\lambda)\det(C-\lambda I) $$ where the first equality uses the fact that similar matrices have the same characteristic polynomial.

Using the fact that $C$ is Hermitian, we can perform this process again on $C$. This allows us to say that $C$ has an orthogonal eigenvector basis, i.e. $$\mrm{diag}(\lambda_{r+1},\lambda_{r+2},\ldots,\lambda_n) = R\inv CR $$ where $R$ is the unitary change-of-basis matrix and $\lambda_{r+1}, \ldots, \lambda_n$ are copies of $\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_r$.

Finally, we can construct the change-of-basis matrix to perform these two changes of basis at once: $$ Q \equiv \pmat{I_r & 0 \\ 0 & R} = \pmat{ 1 & 0 & \ldots & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & \ldots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \ldots & 1 \\ &&&& R_{11} & R_{12} & \ldots & R_{1(n-r)} \\ &&&& R_{21} & R_{22} & \ldots & R_{2(n-r)} \\ &&&& \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ &&&& R_{(n-r)1} & R_{(n-r)2} & \ldots & R_{(n-r)(n-r)} \\ } $$ with $Q$ being unitary. This allows us to change the basis of $H$ to a diagonal matrix $$\begin{align*} Q\inv(P\inv HP)Q = \pmat{\mrm{diag}(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n) & 0 \\ 0 & \mrm{diag}(\lambda_{r+1},\ldots,\lambda_n)} = \mrm{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n). \end{align*}$$

Thus, the matrix $PQ$ changes the basis such that $H$ becomes diagonal. As $P,Q$ are unitary, $PQ$ are unitary as well, so columns of $PQ$ are orthogonal. As eigenvectors are the columns of $PQ$, we have obtained $n$ orthogonal eigenvectors.
