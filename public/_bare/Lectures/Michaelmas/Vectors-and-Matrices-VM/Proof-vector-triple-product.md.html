$$\begin{align*} [\vec a\times(\vec b\times\vec c)]_i &= \epsilon_{ijk} a_j (\vec b\times\vec c)_k \\ &= \epsilon_{ijk} \epsilon_{kpq} a_j b_p c_q \\ &= \epsilon_{ijk} \epsilon_{pqk} a_j b_p c_q \\ &= (\delta_{ip} \delta_{jq} - \delta_{iq} \delta_{jp}) a_j b_p c_q \\ &= a_j (\delta_{ip}b_p) (\delta_{jq}c_q) - a_j (\delta_{jp} b_p) (\delta_{iq}c_q) \\ &= a_j b_i c_j - a_j b_j c_i \\ &= (\vec a\cdot\vec c) b_i - (\vec a\cdot\vec b) c_i \end{align*}$$ Therefore $$ \vec a\times(\vec b\times\vec c) = (\vec a\cdot\vec c)\vec b - (\vec a\cdot\vec b)\vec c. $$

Of course we can prove the second statement by $\vec a\times\vec b=-\vec b\times\vec a$, but that's boring.

$$\begin{align*} [(\vec a\times\vec b)\times\vec c]_i &= \epsilon_{ijk} (\vec a\times\vec b)_j c_k \\ &= \epsilon_{ijk} \epsilon_{jpq} a_p b_q c_k \\ &= \epsilon_{ijk} \epsilon_{qjp} a_p b_q c_k \\ &= (\delta_{iq}\delta_{kp} - \delta_{ip}\delta_{kq})a_p b_q c_k \\ &= a_k b_i c_k - a_i b_k c_k \\ &= (\vec a\cdot\vec c)b_i - (\vec b\cdot\vec c)a_i \end{align*}$$ so $$ (\vec a\times\vec b)\times\vec c = (\vec a\cdot\vec c)\vec b - (\vec b\cdot\vec c)\vec a. $$
